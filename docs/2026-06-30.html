1. Venezuela anger mounts over slow quake rescue

Residents and families in La Guaira are furious at what they describe as a negligent government response to twin earthquakes that have killed at least 1,700 people. At one collapsed 12-storey building, families waited as rescuers drilled through concrete slabs listening for survivors, while relatives said precious days had been wasted before serious efforts began. Kevin Montilla, whose wife and 16-year-old daughter were inside the building when the quake struck, said: "The rescue operation started very late and it's been slow. Initially it was only people who live in the community who came in to help. The police just came to check, but they didn't help." At the nearby Bello Horizonte complex, volunteers with spades and crowbars were digging through rubble while police stood nearby without engaging in rescue efforts.

Why it matters: There are parts of La Guaira state where search teams have so far not even reached, raising fears that the true scale of the disaster may never be known.

Source: BBC

2. Venezuela's US-backed government faces quake test

Critics say the response from Interim President Delcy Rodriguez's government to the earthquakes has been slow and inept, leaving people in the disaster zone largely to save themselves. In Los Corales, neighbourhood volunteers pulled more than a dozen corpses from a collapsed building but, lacking body bags, resorted to garbage bags and plastic sheets. Venezuela's thousands of police and army troops have been slow to arrive, and some have been accused of looting, while others set up roadblocks demanding permits from doctors and rescue workers. Retired Venezuelan Army Gen. Antonio Rivero says Rodriguez could have immediately deployed the armed forces with trucks, generators, portable lights and water systems, but that did not happen.

Why it matters: A Latin America specialist warns that earthquakes can upend governments, citing Nicaragua's Anastasio Somoza, whose regime began to collapse after he and his cronies stole relief aid following a 1972 earthquake.

Source: NPR

3. Pakistani strikes kill 36 civilians in Afghanistan

Pakistani forces' ground operations and airstrikes killed at least 36 civilians in Afghanistan overnight and wounded more than 160 others, Afghan officials said Monday, as tensions between the two neighbours escalated. Pakistan said the operations targeted militant hideouts in Paktia, Paktika, and Kunar provinces, with its Information Minister Attaullah Tarar saying 29 fighters were killed. Afghan officials condemned the strikes as a "cowardly act of aggression," with one deputy spokesperson saying Afghanistan would respond "in due time." The strikes followed a militant attack on the regional headquarters of the paramilitary Rangers in Karachi that killed three soldiers, which Jamaat-ul-Ahrar claimed responsibility for.

Why it matters: Multiple rounds of talks have failed to secure a lasting ceasefire, and hundreds of people have been killed in cross-border fighting since February, when Afghanistan launched retaliatory strikes after Pakistan carried out airstrikes inside Afghan territory.

Source: NPR

4. Peru's Fujimori wins presidency by slim margin

Peru's conservative Keiko Fujimori has won the 7 June presidential runoff, defeating left-wing candidate Roberto Sanchez by fewer than 50,000 votes out of more than 18 million ballots cast. Sanchez had warned he would not recognise a government headed by his rival, alleging administrative irregularities in the handling of the overseas portion of the vote.

Source: The Guardian

5. Japanese yen hits 40-year low against dollar

The Japanese yen weakened to its lowest level against the US dollar since 1986 on Tuesday, falling to 162.19 per dollar as of 1.27 a.m. ET, according to data from LSEG. Japan's Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama said the government was ready to take "decisive action" against excessive currency moves, including action "confirmed between Japan and the U.S." Between April and May, Japan deployed over 11.7 trillion yen ($72.8 billion) in foreign reserves to prop up the currency. Nomura's North Asia chief investment officer Julia Wang said Japan could intervene in the foreign exchange market, but expects any impact on broader markets to be short-lived.

Why it matters: Wang warned that the yen's broader outlook remains weak because wide interest-rate and real-yield differentials between Japan and the US continue to favour carry trades, putting ongoing downward pressure on the Japanese currency.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

Shetland backs undersea tunnels to replace ferries

The council currently runs ferry services to nine islands, carrying around 750,000 passengers each year on 12 vessels at a cost of 23m pounds per year, but costs have risen sharply and some routes are struggling to meet demand. Engineering firm Cowi's tunnelling timeline sets three years for preliminary work and five years for construction, meaning tunnels could be in place within eight years.

Source: BBC

Bank Rate held at 3.75% in June 2026

The Monetary Policy Committee voted 7-2 to maintain Bank Rate at 3.75% at its meeting ending 17 June 2026. Two members voted to increase Bank Rate by 0.25 percentage points, to 4%.
