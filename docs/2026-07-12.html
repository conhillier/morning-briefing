1. Iran closes Hormuz after third US strike wave

The US launched a third round of strikes on Iran this week after the IRGC attacked a Cyprus-flagged vessel, the MV GFS Galaxy, in the Strait of Hormuz, leaving one civilian crew member missing. US Central Command said it hit 140 Iranian military targets, including missile and drone sites, communication networks, and coastal surveillance locations. Iran's IRGC then declared the strait closed until further notice, warning that any US aggression would be met with severity and that new bases in the region would be targeted. Iran's parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf wrote on X: "We told you: keep your word or pay the price. Reality is knocking."

Why it matters: The closure of the Strait of Hormuz represents a major escalation; the US has demanded Iran publicly state the waterway is open and pledge to stop firing on commercial ships before talks can resume.

Source: BBC

2. Iran's new supreme leader vows revenge after closure

His father and predecessor, Ali Khamenei, was killed in an air strike on 28 February, on the first day of the US-Israeli war against Iran, and was buried in his home city of Mashhad on Friday.

Why it matters: The new supreme leader's public vow of revenge and the IRGC's closure of the strait signal that Iran's leadership is hardening its stance, complicating US-mediated efforts to revive negotiations.

Source: BBC

3. Gulf states struck as Iran fires back again

Iran apparently responded to the latest US strikes with attacks targeting Bahrain, Qatar and the United Arab Emirates. The UAE warned the public Sunday of an incoming missile and drone attack as explosions could be heard in nearby Qatar, and Qatar's military said it intercepted the incoming Iranian fire. Missile alerts sounded in Bahrain, home to the US Navy's 5th Fleet.

Why it matters: The spread of Iranian missile and drone strikes to the UAE, a country not previously targeted in this latest round, marks a geographic widening of the conflict in the Gulf.

Source: NPR

4. Big tech carbon emissions hit third of France's output

Microsoft, Amazon and Google's collective carbon emissions have increased by nearly a fifth in the past year, driven largely by datacentre construction, reaching 119m mTCO2e in the financial year ending March 2026 - about a third of the emissions of France. Microsoft's emissions rose by 25% to 20m mTCO2e, "driven primarily by the expansion of our datacentre infrastructure", while Google reported an 18% increase and Amazon a 16% overall rise. The world's biggest tech companies are on track to spend $765bn (£570bn) this year, mostly on building AI datacentres. All three companies say they still aim to achieve net zero emissions, with Google and Microsoft targeting 2030 and Amazon targeting 2040.

Why it matters: Academics warn that as more companies migrate to cloud services, the carbon footprint of AI and digital technology is being obscured and effectively outsourced to the major tech firms.

Source: The Guardian

5. Meta pulls AI image tool after privacy backlash

Meta has abruptly taken down a new feature that allowed people to use its AI tool to make fake images from user content on Instagram, after days of backlash over privacy concerns. The feature was part of a broad rollout of Muse Image, released on Tuesday, and allowed users to tag public-facing accounts and quickly use content from those accounts to create AI-generated or altered images. Instagram users were opted in by default, meaning anyone with a public account could have their likeness used without their knowledge or permission.

Why it matters: Meta admitted it had "missed the mark" and said the feature was "no longer available", though it had already announced plans to bring further AI features and integrations to WhatsApp, Facebook and Messenger.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Murder arrest made in Ann Widdecombe case

A 28-year-old white British man has been arrested on suspicion of the murder of former Conservative minister and Reform UK member Ann Widdecombe, police said on Saturday. The suspect was arrested at an address in the South Yorkshire area, with Devon and Cornwall police supported by Counter Terrorism Policing North East and South Yorkshire police. Widdecombe, 78, was found dead at her bungalow in Haytor, Devon, at 11.40am on Thursday; police believe she was attacked at about 12.30pm on Wednesday. Police said there is "no information to suggest that this is a terrorism-related incident" and that there was no wider risk to the public.

Source: The Guardian
