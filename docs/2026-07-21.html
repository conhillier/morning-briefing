1. US launches 10th night of Iran strikes

The US military launched its 10th consecutive night of strikes against Iran, targeting "military command centres, maritime capabilities, missile and drone launch sites, and air defense systems", according to US Central Command. Iran responded by striking two oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz and claiming hits on US military assets in Bahrain and Kuwait, while President Masoud Pezeshkian said Iran was now fighting a "full-scale war" with the US. The UK's Maritime Trade Operations agency said a tanker in the Strait of Hormuz had reported being struck by an unknown projectile, forcing its crew to abandon the vessel and board a lifeboat.

Why it matters: Tehran said diplomatic messages were still being exchanged by both sides through intermediaries despite the collapse of a ceasefire, leaving the path to de-escalation uncertain.

Source: BBC

2. Trump hits Canada with 50% tariffs

US President Donald Trump has imposed a 50% tariff on a wide range of goods imported from Canada, citing what he called "unequal treatment" of US cars, dairy and alcohol. The new duties, introduced under Section 338 of the 1930 Tariff Act, will take effect in 30 days and apply to all covered goods regardless of whether they fall under the existing USMCA free trade agreement, though energy, potash, critical minerals and fish are spared. Prime Minister Mark Carney said Canada stood ready to "intensify" trade talks with the US in the coming weeks, calling the move "the latest in a series of unilateral US trade actions" that violated the USMCA. The head of the Canadian Chamber of Commerce called the tariffs a "regrettable decision" and urged officials to make "meaningful progress" in talks before the duties take effect.

Why it matters: Chris Swonger, head of the Distilled Spirits Council of the United States, warned the decision "raises the risk of further retaliation", signalling the trade war between the two neighbours could deepen further.

Source: BBC

3. European gas prices hit four-month high

Analysts at ICIS said just 26 LNG cargoes had managed to cross east out of the Gulf since the conflict began on 28 February, compared with the usual 90 to 100 each month, and cut their forecast for global LNG supply this year from 441 million tonnes to 431 million. Brent crude briefly breached the $90 a barrel mark before easing after Iran said diplomatic exchanges were continuing.

Why it matters: ICIS said if gas prices stayed at around €60 a MWh it could mean "potentially costly state intervention to safeguard security of supply", though its modelling suggested European storage could still reach targets by late November.

Source: The Guardian

4. Sinaloa cartel boss Zambada jailed for life

Ismael "El Mayo" Zambada, co-founder of Mexico's Sinaloa Cartel, has been sentenced to life in prison by US District Judge Brian Cogan at a hearing in Brooklyn federal court, two years after being lured to the US in an elaborate sting operation. The 76-year-old was also ordered to pay $15bn (£11bn), representing drug-trafficking profits from his role as principal leader of a criminal enterprise, according to the justice department.

Why it matters: US Attorney Joseph Nocella Jr said "that chapter closes for good", though prosecutors noted the Sinaloa Cartel has been at war with itself since Zambada's arrest, with hundreds of people reported missing during fighting between its two rival factions.

Source: BBC

5. Anthropic's $1.5bn copyright settlement approved

A federal judge has given final approval to Anthropic's $1.5 billion settlement of a class action copyright lawsuit brought by authors and book publishers, in what is believed to be the largest settlement in the history of US copyright law. Judge Araceli Martinez-Olguin signed off on the settlement on Monday; the payout will deliver $3,000 per work across an estimated 500,000 works, shared among authors and publishers who hold rights to them. The case centred on Anthropic building its training library partly from books downloaded from pirate sites including Library Genesis and Pirate Library Mirror, which a previous judge found illegal, even after ruling that training an AI model on copyrighted text counts as fair use. The settlement closes the case but does not create binding precedent, as Anthropic's decision to settle means the ruling will never reach an appeals court.

Why it matters: Copyright lawsuits against Google, Meta, Midjourney, and OpenAI over AI training data remain ongoing, meaning other judges are still free to reach their own conclusions on the core legal questions.

Source: TechCrunch

Closer to Home

Burnham keeps Alexander as Scottish Secretary

Andy Burnham confirmed Douglas Alexander will remain as Secretary of State for Scotland after Burnham unveiled his new cabinet on Monday, with a host of Starmer-era ministers including Peter Kyle, Rachel Reeves, and David Lammy departing. Alexander, who said he was "very happy" to keep his job, said Burnham's desire to "decentralise power to enable communities to shape their own futures" would "chime with Scottish sentiment as much as the rest of the UK". First Minister John Swinney said his government would "engage constructively" on the devolution of responsibilities to Scotland and would push Burnham for the power to hold a second independence referendum, though Burnham has confirmed he will maintain the UK government's opposition to a second poll. Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar said he was confident Burnham would deliver on the change "our country so desperately needs".

Source: BBC
