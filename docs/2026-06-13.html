1. SpaceX debut makes Musk first trillionaire

Elon Musk became the world's first trillionaire on Friday as SpaceX shares soared during its stock market debut on the Nasdaq. The company set its IPO price at $135 per share, but trading opened at $150 and briefly reached $176.50, with shares closing at about $161. SpaceX listed with a value of $2.2tn, and the IPO raised $75bn from investors and underwriters. Musk's total net worth stood at $1.11tn according to the Bloomberg rich list, with his SpaceX shares alone worth $767.1bn at close of trade.

Why it matters: SpaceX is currently not profitable, having lost more than $9bn in 2025 and 2026 so far, meaning its valuation is largely based on optimism about future earnings rather than demonstrated financial results.

Source: BBC

2. Iran deal close but not certain, US says

A senior Trump administration official said Friday that the US and Iran could sign a deal in the "next few days" but that the US is not "100%" confident it will be consummated.

Why it matters: The deal as it stands would reopen the Strait of Hormuz, impose an inspection regime on Iran, end Iran's funding of proxy groups, and offer Iran significant economic relief including loosened sanctions and unfrozen assets, but only if Iran delivers on its commitments.

Source: CNBC

3. Iran deal details: Hormuz reopening, nuclear talks

Iran's Foreign Minister Seyed Abbas Araghchi told state TV that a deal with the US to end fighting is close and includes reopening the Strait of Hormuz and lifting a US blockade of Iran, with talks on Iran's nuclear programme to begin later. US officials confirmed the deal would involve a staged reintegration of Iran into the global economy, with sanctions relief and potential unfreezing of assets happening incrementally, and only when Iran's compliance could be verified. The deal also calls on Iran to stop funding proxy groups in the region, including Hezbollah. Araghchi said there are "supporters and opponents" of the latest terms within Iran's Supreme National Security Council, and that "a collective decision had not been reached."

Why it matters: US officials stressed the MOU is based on "performance" rather than trust, meaning Iran receives economic benefits only when it can be verified it has implemented its commitments, and variations of this agreement have fallen away at late stages before.

Source: BBC

4. US government shuts down Anthropic's top AI models

The US government on Friday ordered Anthropic to immediately shut off access to two of its most powerful AI models, Claude Fable 5 and Claude Mythos 5, citing national security concerns. Anthropic said it received the directive at 5:21 pm ET and has complied, though it made clear it disagrees with the decision. The government's action is framed as an export control order, but Anthropic says its understanding is that the underlying concern is a claimed jailbreak of Fable 5 - specifically a "potential narrow, non-universal jailbreak" that amounts to prompting the model to identify software flaws in a specific codebase.

Why it matters: Mythos had been restricted to roughly 50 vetted organisations including Amazon, Apple, Google, Microsoft and CrowdStrike for defensive cybersecurity work, while Fable 5 had only been released to the public three days ago as a safer commercial version - making the shutdown a significant blow to Anthropic ahead of its widely expected IPO.

Source: TechCrunch

5. Yoon sentenced to 30 years for drone flights

South Korea's ousted President Yoon Suk Yeol and his former defence minister Kim Yong Hyun were sentenced to 30 years in prison on Friday by the Seoul Central District Court. The court found them guilty of aiding an adversary and abusing their power, ruling that they sought to provoke North Korea into launching armed attacks or serious provocations against South Korea to manufacture a national emergency justifying martial law. The court said the moves harmed South Korea's military interests by exposing its capabilities, undermining its ability to conduct future operations and prompting North Korea to strengthen its defence posture. Yoon had previously been sentenced to life in prison by the same court for a rebellion conviction over his short-lived imposition of martial law in December 2024.

Why it matters: Yoon's lawyers said a guilty verdict would undermine South Korea's security interests, arguing the drone flights were a response to North Korea flying thousands of trash-carrying balloons into the South in 2024; they did not immediately say whether they would appeal.

Source: NPR

Closer to Home

UK bans Russian fuel imports by January 2027

The UK government has committed to banning imports of diesel and jet fuel made from Russian oil by 1 January 2027, as part of its sanctions package on Moscow. Trade Minister Chris Bryant said the end date is "a clear signal that we continue to ratchet up maximum pressure on Russia," with the temporary licence to be reviewed every two weeks and potentially revoked sooner. Global oil prices have been pushed up by the US-Iran conflict, with the Brent crude benchmark rising from around $70 a barrel before the conflict to around $87 as a deal to end the fighting appears close. Longstanding Putin critic Sir Bill Browder called the arrangement "absurd," arguing the UK was simultaneously giving Ukraine billions to fight Russia while giving Russia billions for fuel to buy weapons to attack Ukraine.

Source: BBC
