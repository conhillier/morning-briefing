Sunday 21 June 2026 · 4-minute read

1. US-Iran talks open as Hormuz dispute flares

US Vice-President JD Vance landed in Switzerland early Sunday morning for a new round of negotiations with Iran. An Iranian delegation including parliamentary speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf and Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi arrived late on Saturday. Officials from the US and Iran will be joined at the talks by Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif and the head of the country's armed forces, Field Marshal Asim Munir. Complicating the talks, Iran's Islamic Revolution Guard Corps said the Strait of Hormuz had been closed again, citing Israeli strikes on Lebanon as a violation of the US-Iran deal, though US Central Command said "traffic continues to flow" and that 55 merchant ships transited the strait on Saturday.

Why it matters: Vance said he hoped to make progress "on the nuclear issue" and on the "Lebanon ceasefire issue", meaning the talks carry high stakes for the broader regional settlement.

Source: BBC

2. Lebanon death toll mounts as ceasefire falters

Israeli airstrikes on Saturday killed at least 32 people, including women, children, and a Lebanese army soldier, after waves of Israeli attacks on Friday killed 83 people including first responders, according to Lebanon's health ministry. The Lebanese government recorded a grim milestone of at least 4,000 people killed in Israeli attacks since March 2, when the war began. Israel says 35 soldiers and a military contractor, along with two civilians, have been killed in Hezbollah attacks, with almost all the soldiers killed in Lebanon. Hezbollah maintained that a ceasefire does not mean allowing Israeli occupation to remain on Lebanese territory.

Why it matters: Iran cited the ongoing Israeli strikes as the reason it declared the Strait of Hormuz closed again, directly linking the Lebanon fighting to the fragile US-Iran diplomatic process.

Source: NPR

3. Edinburgh anti-Muslim attacks: man charged

A 36-year-old white Scottish man was charged following a series of attacks across Edinburgh on Friday night in which five men were injured. Three other men were allegedly attacked in the Telford Road and Leith Walk area, with posts on social media appearing to show a shirtless man carrying a long weapon battering a restaurant door. Police said none of the injuries were life-threatening and that there was no further threat to the public.

Why it matters: Prime Minister Keir Starmer said the suspect appeared to be motivated by "anti-Muslim hatred" and vowed he would "face the full force of the law", while the Muslim Engagement and Development group urged police to treat it as "Islamophobic, far-right terror".

Source: The Guardian

4. Bedford crash: nine critical, toll rises to 100

Nine people are in a critical condition after the Bedford train crash that killed the driver of one of the trains, with the total number seriously injured rising to 32, according to the East of England ambulance service. The number with minor injuries rose to 57, bringing the total injured to 100. Survivor Brett Byatt, a teacher from Bedford, said the train was not going at full speed and estimated they were only about five minutes into their journey when the collision happened, adding that he believed 90% of passengers in his carriage were injured. The ambulance service transferred 64 patients to hospital in total.

Source: The Guardian

5. Lloyds hires 300 AI experts, job cuts loom

Lloyds Banking Group has launched an AI recruitment drive for 300 tech experts, with the bank intending recruits to work on its use and development of agentic AI by September. The recruits will be part of a 1,000-strong AI team also made up of retrained Lloyds staff, deploying existing large language models such as Anthropic's Claude and building on top of public LLMs such as Google's Gemini. Lloyds's AI programme has already delivered financial gains, with generative AI providing a £50m boost to its balance sheet last year, and the group expects a £100m benefit this year. While the hiring drive will increase headcount for now, the group did not rule out its broad adoption of AI leading to job cuts in the future.

Why it matters: Group data and AI science head Trystan Davies said "AI will reshape how organisations are structured" and that it "will change roles and how we work", signalling further workforce changes ahead.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Edinburgh attacks: counter-terror investigators deployed

Counter-terrorism officers were brought in after a 36-year-old white Scottish man was charged following a series of attacks across Edinburgh on Friday night that left five men injured. The Scottish Association of Mosques said two worshippers were attacked in a park after leaving the Broomhouse mosque, with three other men allegedly attacked on Telford Road and Leith Walk. Scotland's First Minister John Swinney said he was "deeply concerned" by the incidents, adding "there is no place for violence, racism or intolerance in our country". Police said there was no further threat to the public and a report had been submitted to the Procurator Fiscal.

Source: The Guardian
