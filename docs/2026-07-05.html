1. Ukraine strikes St Petersburg oil terminal

Ukraine struck a major oil terminal in Russia's second city of St Petersburg overnight, with President Volodymyr Zelensky describing it as key "infrastructure that generates revenue for Russia's war". The BBC verified that the terminal was hit; Ukraine's military described it as "one of the largest" in Russia, capable of producing 12.5 million tonnes of petroleum products per year. Ukraine also said a key naval base of the Russian Baltic Fleet in Kronstadt was hit. St Petersburg Governor Aleksandr Beglov said 72 Ukrainian drones were shot down over the city and the wider Leningrad region, and urged residents to stay indoors.

Why it matters: Zelensky said the targets hit in St Petersburg and the surrounding region were about 850km (528 miles) from Ukraine's border, and Kyiv says nearly 43% of Russia's oil refining capacity has been "disabled" as a result of its intensified long-range drone attacks on energy infrastructure.

Source: BBC

2. Le Pen verdict to decide presidential race fate

France awaits a Tuesday appeal court ruling that will determine whether Marine Le Pen, the 57-year-old leader of the National Rally, can stand in next year's presidential election. Last year a Paris court found Le Pen guilty of misusing European parliamentary funds and sentenced her to two years imprisonment and five years ineligibility from public office, with the ineligibility declared immediately effective. If the appeal court follows the state advocate's position, which again calls for five years ineligibility, Le Pen will be out of the presidential race. Should she be blocked, the RN's candidate would automatically become 30-year-old Jordan Bardella.

Why it matters: Latest opinion polls suggest Le Pen is well-positioned to become France's next head of state, meaning the court's decision carries what her own lawyer described as "dizzying significance" for the country's political future.

Source: BBC

3. Sudan's El Obeid pummelled by RSF drone strikes

El Obeid, a city of half a million people in Sudan, has suffered escalating drone strikes by the Rapid Support Forces (RSF), with aid workers describing the past weekend's attacks as the most violent yet - hitting schools and fuel stations and killing more than 20 people including students. At least 45 people were killed and 41 injured in 15 drone strikes in the city and surrounding areas from 6 June to 28 June, according to the UN human rights office. The conflict monitoring group Acled recorded 27 drone strikes last month around El Obeid, the highest monthly total since the conflict began in 2023.

Source: The Guardian

4. Stargate UK investment was largely hypothetical

The Guardian has revealed that OpenAI does not appear to have visited one of Stargate UK's key sites, and that £20bn of the "potential" £30bn in investment touted by the UK government appears to have been "totally hypothetical". A freedom of information request showed that neither OpenAI nor Nscale ever met with local authorities at the planned datacentre site at Cobalt Park in North Tyneside; only Nvidia appears to have visited the North East combined authority, doing so in February 2026, five months after Trump's visit. The government said the £20bn figure represented the amount of money the site would need in order to build a datacentre and obtain the necessary computing power - in other words, the government suggested the site would attract £20bn because it needed £20bn.

Why it matters: The findings raise questions about one of the most-hyped UK AI developments and suggest a centrepiece of US-UK AI cooperation was, in one source's words, "little more than a press release", with the plans having been paused in April over concerns about regulation and high energy costs.

Source: The Guardian

5. China promotes two generals after corruption purge

China's military promoted two officers to the rank of general at a ceremony on Friday, in what may be a precursor to a reorganisation at the top following the removal of several leaders in a long-running anti-corruption drive. The promotions could put them in line to fill vacancies on the seven-member commission, which has been effectively reduced to two by the corruption investigations - its only active members being Xi and Vice Chair Zhang Shengmin. A new commission is expected to be announced in the fall of next year, at the end of the current commission's five-year term.

Why it matters: The shake-up is believed to be in part an effort to ensure the military's loyalty to China's ruling Communist Party and its leader, Xi Jinping.

Source: NPR

Closer to Home

Scottish firefighters aid Venezuela quake rescue

Three firefighters from Scotland - Gavin Brown, Ian Hodgson and Brian Richards - have been deployed to Venezuela as part of the UK's search and rescue operation following twin earthquakes that struck on 24 June, killing more than 2,000 people with tens of thousands still missing. The UK crew totals 68 firefighters and staff from 14 fire and rescue services, equipped with sound detectors, vibration sensors and drones to locate survivors, as well as the ability to tunnel into buildings. Ian Hodgson, a crew commander based at North Anderson Drive station in Aberdeen, said the crew uses listening devices to detect "saveable lives" and identify how far away a person is and what resources are required. Brian Richards, a firefighter at MacAlpine Road station in Dundee, said resilience was vital during long days and extreme temperatures, and noted that poor signal in destroyed areas made navigation unexpectedly challenging.

Source: BBC
