1. Iran and Israel pause strikes, deal close

Iran and Israel say they have halted attacks on each other after exchanging fire for the first time since April's truce. Israel's Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said his country was holding fire "at the moment", while Iran's military said it had stopped operations following the delivery of a "painful response" to Israel. An Israeli official said Israel had halted its strikes at Trump's request, and Trump told journalists on Tuesday: "We're in the final throes of what will be a very, very good deal." Trump said a deal could take "two or three days" and that the Strait of Hormuz would open immediately after.

Why it matters: Trump warned Netanyahu that he might "be on your own very soon" if he went back to war with Iran, signalling US pressure is a key factor holding the fragile pause together.

Source: BBC

2. Major earthquake kills 35 in southern Philippines

At least 35 people have died after a magnitude-7.8 earthquake struck off the coast of Mindanao island in the southern Philippines at 07:37 local time on Monday. The quake triggered tsunami alerts in the Philippines, Indonesia, Japan and Australia, though the warnings were later cancelled or downgraded. Officials reported at least 134 injured and some 10,000 families displaced, and more than 130 aftershocks with magnitudes ranging from 1.3 to 6.7 were recorded.

Why it matters: Casualty numbers still need to be verified by the national disaster agency, which is expected to provide an official update in the coming days, meaning the death toll could rise further.

Source: BBC

3. DRC Ebola deaths reach 101 with 550 cases

The Democratic Republic of the Congo has reported 101 confirmed Ebola deaths and 550 confirmed cases in total, with 35 new cases and 10 deaths recorded in the previous 24 hours alone. Unlike the Zaire strain, there is currently no approved vaccine or treatment for the Bundibugyo strain, and two potential vaccine candidates are not yet ready for human trials.

Why it matters: Neighbouring Uganda has recorded 19 cases and two deaths, and one confirmed case involved a Congolese citizen who had travelled to the United Arab Emirates before entering Uganda, raising fears of wider international spread.

Source: Al Jazeera

4. OpenAI files for stock market listing

OpenAI has made a confidential filing with the US Securities and Exchange Commission to pursue an initial public offering at some point in the future, the company confirmed on Monday. The filing comes exactly one week after rival AI firm Anthropic said it was also planning to go public. OpenAI's most recent private valuation stood at $852bn, while Anthropic's most recent valuation hit $965bn.

Why it matters: OpenAI's compute costs are estimated to be over $100 billion dollars a year, while its revenue is a fraction of that, making an IPO a potential route to the capital needed to sustain operations.

Source: BBC

5. Apple overhauls Siri as Tim Cook exits

Apple announced a significant overhaul of its digital assistant at its annual Worldwide Developers Conference (WWDC) on Monday, unveiling Siri AI, which the company promised would offer a better artificial intelligence experience for users. The new version of Siri will draw from a user's past interactions, an understanding of images, and broad-world knowledge, and will feature a new standalone app. WWDC is set to be Tim Cook's last as CEO before he steps down in September after 15 years at the helm, with John Ternus named as his replacement.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Scottish schools cut subjects in teacher crisis

Secondary schools across Scotland are reducing timetables and cutting whole subjects from the curriculum due to a lack of specialist teachers, education leaders have told BBC Scotland News. The Association of Directors of Education in Scotland says there are particular shortages in maths, science, design technology and computing, and its president Laurence Findlay described the situation as a "persistent and worsening shortage" that had become a "crisis". BBC Scotland News analysis found that more than 4,000 spaces on the secondary postgraduate teacher training course (PGDE) have not been filled in the past nine years, with the most dramatic shortfalls in maths, English, languages and sciences. At Mackie Academy in Stonehaven, the school advertised twice for a design technology teacher and received no applicants, meaning the subject will not be on offer to S1 pupils next year.

Source: BBC
