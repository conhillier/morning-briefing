1. US lifts Iran naval blockade after deal signed

The US has dropped its naval blockade of Iran after the two countries signed a deal to end the war in the Middle East. US Central Command confirmed the end of the blockade on X "in accordance with the President's direction", and said some US vessels would remain "in the general area". Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei said he had approved the deal despite having a "different view", after assurances from President Masoud Pezeshkian that he would "protect the rights of the Iranian nation". Khamenei said President Trump had "out of desperation, used all kinds of leverage" to bring the deal about.

Why it matters: The US-Iran deal centres around 14 core points, including the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz and a requirement that Iran should never have a nuclear weapon, but both Israel and Hezbollah have carried out strikes against each other since the agreement was announced, including strikes in Lebanon on Thursday that killed three people.

Source: BBC

3. Ukraine hits Moscow refinery in record drone barrage

The Kapotnya refinery in south-east Moscow was hit for the third time in a month and the second time this week, and 17 people were wounded in the Moscow region according to local governor Andrei Vorobyov. Moscow's four airports were temporarily shut and more than 500 flights were cancelled or delayed. Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky said the strike was an answer to last week's Russian attack on Kyiv which set ablaze the Pechersk Lavra monastery, saying "if Ukraine burns, your Moscow will burn too."

Source: BBC

4. UK May borrowing surges well past forecasts

May's borrowing was £5.6bn higher than the Office for Budget Responsibility's forecast, which had been made in March before the impact of the war in the Middle East had become clear. The Office for National Statistics said interest payable on government debt jumped to £11.7bn, described as the highest ever recorded in any May. Capital Economics said the higher-than-expected borrowing highlights "the fragile fiscal backdrop that will face whoever is in 10 Downing Street."

Why it matters: The figures came as Andy Burnham was elected MP for Makerfield in a by-election, paving the way for him to launch a leadership challenge against Keir Starmer, with Capital Economics warning the borrowing overshoot will constrain whoever is Prime Minister following a potential leadership challenge.

Source: BBC

5. SpaceX post-IPO buyers nearly break even after slide

Shares of SpaceX fell 3.6% Thursday to just under $184.98 a share, with the stock's five-day volume-weighted average price at $181.71, suggesting the average post-IPO buyer is now approximately breaking even. The stock had soared from its $135 IPO price to an intraday high above $225 on Tuesday before retreating 20%, wiping out much of the gains accumulated after the debut. The reversal has narrowed profits for thousands of retail investors who gained access to the IPO through brokerage platforms including Robinhood, Fidelity and SoFi, though those who received allocations at the $135 offering price still hold gains. After briefly pushing SpaceX's market value close to $3 trillion, investors have begun reassessing whether the stock's rapid advance can be justified by fundamentals.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

Scottish Tories win Westminster seat for first time in 50 years

The Scottish Conservatives have won a Westminster by-election for the first time in more than 50 years, taking Aberdeen South from the SNP with Tory MSP Douglas Lumsden defeating SNP candidate Richard Thomson by a margin of more than 6,000 votes, with the Tories taking almost half of all ballots cast. The SNP held Arbroath and Broughty Ferry, where Lara Bird won the seat with a majority of more than 5,000 votes over the Conservatives. Lumsden, a former oil and gas worker, said his constituents had sent a message that "the destruction of the oil and gas industry must stop now". The Conservatives last won a Westminster by-election north of the border in 1973, when they held Edinburgh North.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts reforms delayed to April 2028

Small companies and micro-entities will be required to file profit and loss accounts with Companies House but may opt out of publication on the public register. All UK registered companies must file accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software from April 2028, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings on that date. The implementation date has shifted from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
