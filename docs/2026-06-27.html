1. Venezuela quake death toll reaches 920

La Guaira, a region north of the capital, has been hit the hardest; at least 243 people have been rescued there, and 172 people are still believed to be trapped. There have been 214 aftershocks since the initial quakes, Acting President Delcy Rodriguez said. Hundreds of international rescue workers have arrived, including a UK military flight from RAF Brize Norton carrying specialists from 14 UK fire services, led by Merseyside Fire and Rescue, as well as teams from the United States, the Netherlands, Mexico, and Switzerland.

Why it matters: The UN's humanitarian chief Tom Fletcher said the disaster "needs an international global response," while the Norwegian Refugee Council's secretary general warned that Venezuela was ill-prepared because of its already "crumbling infrastructure" following decades of underinvestment.

Source: BBC

2. Europe heatwave shatters German record

Germany's highest ever temperature of 41.3C was recorded provisionally in Saarbrucken on Friday, while a provisional June record of 37.1C was set at Cavendish in Suffolk in the UK. Spain's MoMo monitoring system counted 327 fatalities between Sunday and Thursday that could be linked to the heat, and the number of drowning deaths in France since the heatwave began has risen to 55, with an estimated two-thirds occurring in unsupervised areas. Scientists at World Weather Attribution described the current heatwave as "the most severe ever recorded" for the region, finding that June was warming faster than any other month. In Switzerland, the Beznau nuclear power plant took both reactors off grid because the River Aare reached 25C, considered too high to cool the reactors sufficiently.

Why it matters: At least 150 million people across the European continent were facing temperatures higher than 35C on Friday, according to AFP, and the World Meteorological Organization warned of "major impacts" to health, ecosystems, agriculture and labour.

Source: BBC

3. OpenAI limits GPT-5.6 rollout at US request

OpenAI is restricting its new GPT-5.6 models to a "small group of trusted partners" at the behest of the US government, the company said Friday. The lineup includes three models: Sol, its flagship; Terra, a more balanced everyday option; and Luna, a faster, lower-cost version. CEO Sam Altman told staff the government would be "approving access customer by customer during this preview period" and that broader release would follow "a couple of weeks later" if the process went well.

Source: The Guardian

4. Trump threatens 100% tariff over digital taxes

President Trump threatened to impose a 100% import tariff on any European country that introduces a digital services tax on American technology companies, writing on Truth Social that "Numerous European countries" were close to implementing such a levy. He warned the penalties would be applied immediately and would "supersede" any existing bilateral trade agreements. The UK has had a 2% Digital Services Tax in place since 2020, which applies to major platforms with global revenues exceeding £500 million and raised more than £800 million in 2024-25, up from £678 million in 2023-24. France, Italy, and Spain each impose a 3% digital services tax on large companies operating in their countries.

Source: BBC

5. US pledges $150m for Venezuela quake relief

The Trump administration announced a $150 million commitment to faith-based aid groups and UN agencies for the Venezuela earthquake response, along with the deployment of a disaster assistance team and two search and rescue teams from Virginia and California. The Pentagon has also been tapped to help get US government staff and critical supplies into the country. Aid organisation Project Hope's Cesar Jimenez said: "We're facing very, very severe emergency.

Why it matters: Former Obama disaster response chief Jeremy Konyndyk said the administration may have learned lessons from its failure to respond in Myanmar, and noted that Venezuela is a country of geopolitical interest to the US after it toppled Nicolás Maduro in January.

Source: NPR

Closer to Home

Fugitive rapist Rossi dies in US hospital

Nicholas Rossi, the American rapist who faked his own death, fled to Scotland, and was arrested in a Glasgow hospital in 2021, has died aged 38 in a US hospital. The Utah Department of Corrections said he died on Thursday, 25 June 2026, after he "chose to remove himself from the care that was being provided" for chronic, degenerative conditions. Rossi had been serving 10 years to life after being convicted in two separate trials in 2024 of raping two women in Utah in 2008. Salt Lake County Attorney Sim Gill said Rossi "died with the public knowledge of his guilt, and the personal accountability of being in prison."

Source: BBC
