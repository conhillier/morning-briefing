1. US and Iran trade Hormuz strikes

The US launched strikes on Iran after attacks on three oil tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, with US Central Command saying it hit over 80 targets, including more than 60 IRGC small boats in the strait. Iranian state media said strikes hit Qeshm island, Bandar Abbas and Sirik, where people were injured by shrapnel. The IRGC said it retaliated by launching missiles and drones at 85 key US military facilities in Bahrain and Kuwait, including a US Navy headquarters and an air base in Kuwait. Before the strikes, the US Treasury revoked a waiver that had temporarily lifted oil sanctions on Iran, which Iran's foreign ministry called a breach of the memorandum of understanding signed last month.

Why it matters: Iran's foreign ministry warned Tehran 'will take whatever measures it considers necessary to safeguard its national interests and national security', raising fears the fragile ceasefire could collapse entirely.

Source: BBC

2. Oil surges as Hormuz crisis deepens

Brent crude rose as much as 3 percent on Wednesday, with Brent futures for September standing at $76.07 a barrel as of 04:00 GMT, the highest since June 23. The jump reversed a slide that had seen prices return to pre-war levels, following the US strikes on Iran and revocation of the temporary sanctions waiver on Iranian oil. Senior market analyst Tony Sycamore said disagreement between the US and Iran over whether the strait is an international waterway or partly Iran's territorial waters was never fully resolved. Energy researcher Saul Kavonic told Al Jazeera that passage through the strait could remain below 50 percent of pre-war levels for many months with periodic flare-ups in hostilities.

Why it matters: Kavonic said Iran 'fully intends to cement its control over the Strait of Hormuz in the coming weeks', a scenario he described as unacceptable to the US, many Gulf states and global customers.

Source: Al Jazeera

3. NATO allies pledge £37bn missile project

Twelve countries including the UK are set to spend more than £37bn ($50bn) over the next ten years on a new long-range missile called the Deep Precision Strike project, unveiled by Number 10 and due to be discussed at the NATO summit in Ankara on Wednesday. The weapon is intended to strike targets nearly 200 miles (300km) away with pinpoint accuracy, possibly extending out to 1,250 miles, and is billed as one of NATO's most advanced weapons. Sir Keir Starmer said the UK-led initiative would 'help bring European allies together to keep Nato safe for years to come', though the system is not expected to be ready until the 2030s. The government also said NATO had scrambled fighter jets to intercept Russian aircraft approaching allied airspace more than 700 times and that Russian military activity around UK waters had surged 30%.

Why it matters: Foreign Secretary Yvette Cooper said the capability would allow the UK and allies to 'hit high value military targets and the logistical engines that drive armies, deterring any aggressor and strengthening our mutual security.'

Source: BBC

4. Iran war pushes million more into mortgage pain

A million more UK homeowners face higher mortgage bills than the Bank of England had previously expected due to the impact of the Iran war, bringing the total to just over five million who should expect repayments to increase by the end of 2028. The Iran war led to the closure of the Strait of Hormuz shipping lane, typically responsible for around a fifth of global energy supplies, pushing up oil and gas costs, driving inflation and raising the prospect of central banks hiking interest rates. The average two-year fixed rate jumped from 4.83% at the start of March to a peak of 5.90% on 12 April, according to Moneyfacts, and has since dropped to 5.49%. A typical owner-occupier rolling off a fixed rate in the next two years is likely to face an increase of £45 on their monthly mortgage bill, though those paying less than 3% interest and rolling off this year would see an average increase of £170 per month.

Why it matters: The report also warned that borrowers who had been projected to remortgage close to their existing rate are now unlikely to see repayments fall over coming years, as had been forecast prior to the Iran conflict.

Source: BBC

5. PIP system declared 'not fit for purpose'

The government's landmark Timms review of personal independence payments will conclude that disability benefits in England and Wales are 'not fit for purpose' and that the entire assessment system must be redrawn, The Guardian reports. The review, led by disability minister Stephen Timms, found the points-based assessment system is 'in effect worthless' because of the rising number of conditions - particularly mental health - that can fluctuate considerably in severity, and that the process is dehumanising and actively hinders people from seeking work. About 4 million adults now receive PIP, double the number in 2019, and the review received more than 38,000 submissions from members of the disabled community over its 10 weeks, the majority of whom reported negative experiences. The interim review is expected this week, with recommendations on how a new system should be designed due in the autumn.

Why it matters: The review's conclusions make it likely the government will not pursue a harsher points-based system and will instead explore 'much more root-and-branch welfare reform', according to The Guardian.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

John Lewis to cut around 200 jobs

Around 200 John Lewis staff could lose their jobs as the retailer plans to close its in-store money exchange services and dedicated gift wrapping areas, with no final decision made but cuts expected in the autumn if redundancy plans are approved. John Lewis said the decision to close its in-store bureaux de change was due to falling demand, noting customers were increasingly ordering foreign currency online or using credit cards abroad. The money exchange closure will affect 30 shops while the gift wrapping services closure will affect 25 shops. The retailer's latest results show a pre-tax loss of £21m due to £120m worth of one-off costs, though underlying profits rose 6% to £134m and sales across the business rose 5% to £13.4bn.

Source: BBC
