1. US-Iran deal signed, oil prices tumble

Pakistan's Prime Minister Shehbaz Sharif announced a framework deal to end the US-Iran war, with an official signing ceremony scheduled for Friday, 19 June in Switzerland. Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi confirmed on state TV that a deal with the US had been finalised, while Trump posted on social media "let the oil flow!". Brent crude fell 4.3% to $83.55 a barrel on Monday, while US-traded oil was down 4.9% at $80.74. The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed since shortly after the US and Israel launched airstrikes on Iran on 28 February, and Brent crude peaked at about $120 during the war, up from around $70 before the conflict started.

Why it matters: Energy market experts warn that movement of oil through the strait is unlikely to immediately return to pre-war levels, with mines needing to be cleared first - a process that could take from a few weeks to up to six months, according to consulting company Lipow Oil Associates.

Source: BBC

2. Global markets surge on Iran peace deal

US stock futures jumped early Monday after President Trump announced a deal to end the war with Iran, with Dow Jones futures adding 440 points (0.9%), S&P 500 futures climbing 1.14%, and Nasdaq 100 futures rising 1.79%. Asia-Pacific markets led the advance, with South Korea's Kospi rising 5.56% and Japan's Nikkei 225 adding 4.90%. Japanese tech investor SoftBank was the best performer among major Asian tech stocks, rising over 12%, while Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix gained 4.65% and 6.42% respectively. Trump said on Truth Social that "the Deal with the Islamic Republic of Iran is now complete" and authorised the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, sending oil prices tumbling.

Source: CNBC

3. World leaders welcome US-Iran peace deal

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer called the deal a "hugely important" step in ending the war and said "toll-free freedom of navigation must now be restored in the Strait of Hormuz", offering to support mine clearance operations if needed. UN Secretary-General Antonio Guterres praised the deal as a "critical step" towards a "peaceful settlement of the conflict". French President Emmanuel Macron called for the "urgent and unconditional reopening of the Strait of Hormuz", saying the resumption of maritime traffic was "an indispensable condition for regional stability and the global economy". Pakistani PM Sharif congratulated Washington and Tehran for their "commitment to finding a diplomatic solution to the conflict" and thanked Qatar, Saudi Arabia and Turkiye for their contribution.

Source: Al Jazeera

4. Russia strikes Kyiv, damages 11th-century cathedral

A large-scale Russian attack on Ukraine killed five rescuers in Kharkiv and wounded at least 20 people in Kyiv on Monday, as strikes set apartment buildings ablaze and sparked a fire at one of the country's most significant religious landmarks. The roof of the Dormition Cathedral at the Kyiv-Pechersk Lavra - a UNESCO-listed World Heritage site built from the 11th to the 19th century - caught fire during the overnight attack. The rescuers in Kharkiv were killed by a second Russian strike as they fought a blaze caused by an earlier attack, Interior Minister Ihor Klymenko said, with at least five other emergency workers wounded. Five strikes hit civilian sites in Kyiv's Shevchenkivskyi district in less than 30 minutes, including a 25-story apartment building, while a market and a grocery store also caught fire.

Source: NPR

5. Swiss voters reject 10 million population cap

Voters in Switzerland have rejected a proposal from the right-wing Swiss People's Party to limit the country's population to 10 million, with results showing nearly 55% voted against and 45% voted for, on a turnout of 60%. The divisive vote risked putting the country's free movement agreement with the European Union in jeopardy - had the cap been approved, Switzerland would have had to terminate that agreement, through which over half of all Swiss products are sold into the EU. Switzerland's population has grown rapidly since 2002, when it stood at 7.3 million; it is now 9.1 million, with 27% not Swiss citizens. Welcoming the result, Switzerland's justice minister Beat Jans said it was "a sign of stability, openness and reliability", while EU Commission President Ursula von der Leyen said "the EU and Switzerland share deep ties and a strong partnership".

Why it matters: The result preserves Switzerland's crucial access to Europe's single market, but the Swiss People's Party president Marcel Dettling warned that "not a single problem has been solved", signalling that concerns over housing, transport and health costs will persist.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Glasgow Airport strikes averted with pay deals

Summer strikes at Glasgow Airport have been averted after nearly 400 workers accepted pay offers from ICTS and Menzies Aviation, lifting the threat of industrial action during the World Cup and Commonwealth Games. Around 230 ICTS security workers agreed a two-year deal including a 5% increase in basic pay, shift allowances backdated to 1 January, and a one-off bank holiday. A further 160 Menzies Aviation ground services crew will receive a 4.4% increase backdated to January, followed by a minimum 5% rise from 1 July - Unite said this could rise to as much as 11.1% for some workers. However, strike action remains possible at Aberdeen and Edinburgh Airports, with an industrial action ballot involving ICTS HBS security staff remaining open until 17 June and around 370 Edinburgh Airport workers still in dispute.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, all UK registered companies must file annual accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings from that date. Small companies and micro-entities will be required to file profit and loss accounts but may opt out of publication on the public register; abridged accounts will no longer be permitted. The implementation date has shifted from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
