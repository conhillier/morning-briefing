1. US strikes Iranian bridges and airport in sixth night

The US launched a wave of strikes against Iran for the sixth night in a row, its military said, as the two sides battled for control of the Strait of Hormuz. Iran's state media reported that the US had struck civilian infrastructure, including bridges, a train station and an airport, and the BBC verified an attack on one bridge to the west of Bandar Abbas in Hormozgan province. US missiles also struck close to the island of Qeshm, near the strait, as well as in Bandar Abbas and Bushehr - the site of a nuclear power plant. Seven people were killed in the strikes, state news agency IRNA reported.

Why it matters: The International Energy Agency's head Fatih Birol said "We should be worried, and I am worried, if the situation does not improve in the next few weeks", as the Strait of Hormuz - a critical waterway - has remained shut, impacting the flow of oil from the region.

Source: BBC

2. Zelensky fires popular defence minister, protests erupt

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky's surprise move to replace popular Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov has caused an outburst of anger from civil society and protests from MPs. A crowd of mostly young people gathered in Kyiv and other Ukrainian cities on Thursday, holding up signs reading "Hands off Fedorov" and "Stop sabotaging victory!" and chanting "Shame!". Fedorov, 35, revealed he had suggested to Zelensky that Commander-in-Chief Syrskyi and Chief of the General Staff Andrii Hnatov should be replaced, but Zelensky declined, leading to an impasse in which Fedorov said "all the initiatives we proposed were blocked". By evening, Zelensky had put forward Maj-Gen Yevhenii Khmara, the acting head of the Security Service, as acting defence minister.

Why it matters: Zelensky acknowledged that the conflict between the General Staff and the defence ministry had been "systemic" and occurred "at various levels", suggesting deep structural tensions at the heart of Ukraine's war leadership at a critical moment.

Source: BBC

3. British Steel nationalised to protect UK supply

British Steel has been taken into public ownership in a move the government said would protect jobs and safeguard "a vital national capability". The steelworks employs roughly 2,700 people in Scunthorpe and supports many other industries in north Lincolnshire, and the National Audit Office had noted it was costing the government about £1.3m a day. The nationalisation came after Parliament on Wednesday passed legislation allowing the government to bring the steel industry into public ownership under circumstances where it met a public interest test. Jingye, which previously owned the company, is seeking compensation for nationalisation, having previously said the business was losing £700,000 a day.

Why it matters: Business Secretary Peter Kyle warned that the alternative was to "let this business go bust", which would mean the UK losing the ability for primary steel production and becoming entirely dependent on global supply - making the UK the only G7 member without the ability to make virgin steel.

Source: BBC

4. Burnham to promise 'courage to fix big things'

Andy Burnham will pledge he has the "courage to fix the big things that politics has neglected" in his first speech as Labour leader, set to be announced at a special conference on Friday. Burnham will promise to give back control to communities and spread growth across the country, and will take over as prime minister from Keir Starmer on Monday. His Downing Street team is taking shape, with James Purnell, former chief executive of Flint Global, set to be chief of staff, and Alison Phillips, former editor of the Mirror, made transition director. Burnham's new Downing Street team will oversee policy announcements next week, including new details of his policies on energy, water and the cost of living.

Source: The Guardian

5. UK economy returns to 0.1% growth in May

The UK economy returned to growth in May, with the Office for National Statistics reporting GDP rose 0.1%, in line with economists' forecasts, after a 0.1% decline in April. Services output was up 0.3% over the month, but was partly offset by a 0.5% decline in production and a 0.8% fall in construction. The strongest contributor to monthly output was scientific research and development, which was up 5.1%. Over the three months to May, GDP growth was up 0.7%, a modest slowdown from the 0.8% seen in the three months to April.

Why it matters: The British Chambers of Commerce warned that "the Iran conflict is having real-world consequences for UK firms" with rising energy prices and shipping disruption increasing costs, while the Resolution Foundation reckons more than half of the £23.6bn in fiscal headroom Reeves left against her fiscal rules will be wiped out by the effects of the war.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Glasgow preps for Commonwealth Games amid mixed buzz

Glasgow is preparing to welcome the 2026 Commonwealth Games against a backdrop of roadworks, refurbishments and fire damage next to the city's largest train station. About 3,000 athletes will compete for 215 gold medals across the 10-day event, which features 10 sports rather than the 17 it included in 2014, and an estimated 20,000 people are expected to collect accreditation passes before the Games ends on 2 August. Journalist Susan Egelstaff, who competed in badminton at three Commonwealth Games for Scotland, said "there isn't anything like the same buzz as 2014" and characterised the mood as "more apathy than people being actually negative". George Square remains sealed off as work continues on its redevelopment, though organisers stress both Union Street and Central Station will be fully re-opened in time for the Games.

Source: BBC
