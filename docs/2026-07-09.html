1. US hits 90 Iran targets in new strikes

The US military launched a new round of strikes on Iran early Thursday, with US Central Command saying it hit 90 Iranian military targets, including air defence systems and military logistics infrastructure along Iran's coastline. Explosions were reported in the southern port cities of Bandar Abbas, Sirik, Chabahar, and Konarak, with Iranian state TV reporting eight explosions in Bandar Abbas alone. Trump posted on Truth Social that the strikes were 'in retribution for yesterday's bombing of ships by Iran' and warned 'if it happens again, it will get much worse'.

Why it matters: Trump declared the ceasefire agreement signed last month 'over' and said further talks were 'a waste of time', raising fears the broader conflict could resume.

Source: BBC

2. Iran fires back at Gulf Arab states

Thursday's attacks appeared bigger than previous exchanges, with sirens sounding at least twice in Bahrain, home to the US Navy's 5th Fleet headquarters. Kuwait's military said it was actively intercepting incoming drones and missiles, while Qatar issued a security alert. In Iran's southwestern Khuzestan province, at least three people were killed according to state media, following at least nine people killed in Wednesday's strikes. Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Qalibaf warned on X: 'America still hasn't learned that bullying and breaking promises are no longer cost-free. Let me put it plainly: if you strike, you'll get hit.'

Why it matters: A renewed conflict could engulf the wider Middle East and would likely again halt energy shipments through the Strait of Hormuz, through which a fifth of the world's traded oil and natural gas passed before the war began.

Source: NPR

3. Oil and markets rattled by Iran crisis

Brent crude futures for September delivery rose 1.03% to $78.82 a barrel on Thursday, after having risen more than 4% on Wednesday. US West Texas Intermediate futures for August rose 1.06% to $74.29 per barrel.

Why it matters: As the Strait of Hormuz is one of the most important energy chokepoints in the world, 'even limited disruption can have an outsized impact on prompt pricing, freight costs and market sentiment,' according to Saxo.

Source: CNBC

4. Sizewell B gets 20-year life extension

Britain's Sizewell B nuclear power plant in Suffolk has been granted a 20-year life extension, allowing it to continue generating electricity until 2055, having first been synchronised with the National Grid in 1995. The plant produces 3% of Britain's electricity, enough to meet the equivalent demand of 2.5 million homes, and was previously due to shut down within the next decade. Under the deal, owner EDF will receive £70.50 for every megawatt-hour Sizewell B generates starting from 2035, with extra investment coming from Centrica, which owns a 20% share in EDF's UK reactors. The government said the extension is part of a broader ambition for a 'golden age of nuclear' to help meet demand from electric vehicles, low-carbon heating, and AI datacentres.

Why it matters: The government hopes nuclear will help the UK meet growing electricity demand while keeping its climate commitments intact, with new projects at Hinkley Point C and Sizewell C also in the pipeline.

Source: The Guardian

5. PIP review: disability benefits 'not working'

The Timms review of the personal independence payment (PIP) has published its interim report concluding the benefit, claimed by nearly 4 million people in England and Wales, suffers from 'systematic and deep-rooted problems' that have undermined public trust. The report said the onerous application process and hostile eligibility assessments could be so distressing and 'soul destroying' that they led people to give up work and their social lives. People described PIP as something that 'breaks' them rather than a support enabling them to live independently. The review committed to making 'bold and radical proposals' to overhaul the benefit, but indicated there would be 'no blank cheque' and any changes would have to be sustainable within current official PIP spending projections.

Why it matters: The final recommendations, expected to land on the desk of a new prime minister and chancellor in the autumn, will have to balance meaningful reform with political pressure to keep a lid on social security spending.

Source: The Guardian

Firefighters are tackling a large blaze which broke out at a building on Edinburgh's Princes Street in the early hours of Thursday. The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service said the incident was still ongoing, with police also on the scene.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

Small companies and micro-entities will be required to file profit and loss accounts with Companies House but may opt out of publication on the public register. All UK registered companies must file accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software from April 2028, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings on that date. The implementation date has shifted from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
