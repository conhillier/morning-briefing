1. SK Hynix raises $26.5bn in record US listing

South Korean chip maker SK Hynix has raised $26.5bn (£19.8bn) in its New York share offering, marking the largest ever listing by a foreign firm in the US. The company sold 177.9 million American depositary shares at $149 each, with shares set to begin trading Friday on the Nasdaq. Demand for the offering was reportedly over seven times more than the number of shares available, highlighting strong investor appetite.

Why it matters: The US listing gives SK Hynix easier access to huge amounts of potential investment from the world's biggest economy, which has fewer barriers than South Korea, according to Seoul National University finance professor Jaewon Choi.

Source: BBC

2. Hormuz shipping traffic falls sharply after strikes

Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has fallen steeply after a series of tit-for-tat strikes by the US and Iran following an attack on three tankers. Just 23 tankers and cargo ships crossed the critical Gulf waterway on Wednesday, according to maritime intelligence firm Kpler, down from 47 a week before. Before the conflict began, an average of 138 ships crossed through the strait each day, according to the Joint Maritime Information Center. The number of vessels using the US-recommended Omani route has ground to a halt, with no ships using it on Wednesday, falling from just three ships the day before.

Why it matters: More than a fifth of the world's oil and gas supplies, as well as fertiliser shipments and other vital goods, flow through the strait, making the disruption a significant threat to global supply chains.

Source: BBC

3. Ukraine presses ahead on Patriot missile production

President Volodymyr Zelenskyy said Ukraine must make every effort to start domestic production of Patriot interceptor missiles as soon as possible, after political approval was obtained from US President Donald Trump at this week's NATO summit in Ankara.

Why it matters: Global supplies of Patriot interceptors are running low and Ukraine is using them at a faster rate than they can be produced in the US, making domestic production critical for Ukraine's air defences against Russian ballistic missiles.

Source: Al Jazeera

4. OpenAI launches GPT-5.6 model family

OpenAI unveiled its newest family of models on Thursday, introducing GPT-5.6 in three variants: Sol (its workhorse), Terra (intermediate), and Luna (budget-friendly). CEO Sam Altman said Sol is 54% more token efficient when it comes to AI coding tasks, and the company calls 5.6 its "strongest cybersecurity model yet, achieving frontier performance with significantly fewer tokens." The company also released a new tool called ChatGPT Work, designed as a workplace companion for enterprise teams running on desktop, web, and mobile, that can help with tasks like drafting documents, spreadsheets, and presentations. Pricing per million tokens is: Sol at $5 input / $30 output, Terra at $2.50 input / $15 output, and Luna at $1 input / $6 output.

Source: TechCrunch

5. OpenAI No. 2 Fidji Simo steps down

Fidji Simo, OpenAI's No. 2 executive and CEO of Applications, is stepping down from her full-time role due to ongoing medical leave that has "proven longer and harder than expected," transitioning instead to a part-time advisory role. Simo joined OpenAI in May 2025 in a newly created role reporting directly to Sam Altman that consolidated the company's business and product operations, with COO Brad Lightcap, CFO Sarah Friar, and CPO Kevin Weil all beginning to report to her. She first disclosed her health issues in April, announcing she was taking medical leave for a relapse of a neuroimmune condition. Altman responded on X: "i am really sad about this and very grateful for all fidji has done for openai... this sucks."

Why it matters: Simo's departure leaves Altman searching for a successor right as OpenAI, most recently assigned an $852 billion valuation, eyes a possible IPO, creating what the source describes as "a real vacuum for him to address."

Source: TechCrunch

Closer to Home

Edinburgh Princes Street blaze probe under way

Police and fire investigators have launched a joint probe into a blaze that decimated the B-listed former Debenhams store on Edinburgh's Princes Street, with 50 firefighters battling the flames at its height in the early hours of Thursday. The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service said it had "fully extinguished" the flames but crews would remain at the scene for "some time" while dampening down work continues, while entry to the building is still deemed unsafe. Deputy assistant chief officer Willie Pollard said the building "was undergoing construction works and has suffered extensive damage," adding that firefighters' actions helped prevent the spread of fire to neighbouring buildings. Road closures on Princes Street, Rose Street and Rose Street South Lane remain in place, with tram services beginning and ending at the West End.

Source: BBC
