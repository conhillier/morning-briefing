1. Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire struck but strikes continue

A US official confirmed that Israel and Hezbollah have agreed a ceasefire in Lebanon, even as Israeli air strikes killed 47 people including women and children and wounded 97 overnight. Rescue officials in Nabatieh told the BBC there had been at least 12 air strikes since the ceasefire began at 16:00 local time. Hezbollah's secretary general Sheikh Naim Qassem declared: "The project to eliminate Hezbollah has failed, and the Israelis will withdraw from every last inch of our land." Israeli military spokesperson Effie Defrin said Israel would "continue to remove immediate threats, respond to Hezbollah's violations, and do whatever is necessary to protect our civilians".

Why it matters: Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi accused Israel of wanting "permanent war" and insisted that any breach of the commitments set out in the memorandum of understanding "will be attributed to the US", putting the broader US-Iran deal under strain.

Source: BBC

2. Tehran presses Washington over Lebanon ceasefire compliance

Iran says it is ready to move forward with diplomacy with the United States, but insists Washington must ensure Israel complies with the agreement to end the war. Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi said the US has a "commitment and responsibility" to ensure the deal is upheld on all fronts. Analyst Ali Vaez of the International Crisis Group warned that turning the MoU into a broader agreement will be "very difficult" and "very ambitious" within the 60-day timeframe. The Trump administration has announced new talks between Israeli and Lebanese officials in Washington on June 23 and 25, aimed at advancing a "lasting peace".

Source: Al Jazeera

3. Burnham wins Makerfield, leadership challenge looms

Labour's Andy Burnham, the current mayor of Greater Manchester, has won the special election for the seat of Makerfield in northwest England, winning almost 55% of the 45,510 votes counted - more than 9,000 more than Reform UK's Rob Kenyon. The victory cements Burnham's status as the top contender to replace Prime Minister Keir Starmer as Labour leader. In his victory speech, Burnham said: "Everyone knows that politics isn't working. Everyone can feel that the country isn't where it should be. Tonight could, just could, be the turning point." Burnham is likely to seek a meeting with Starmer to argue that the prime minister should exit gracefully and set a timetable for his departure.

Why it matters: Britain's parliamentary system allows governing parties to change leaders midterm, with the winner becoming prime minister without the need for a national election; under Labour rules, a lawmaker can challenge the leader if they have backing from a fifth of the party's House of Commons lawmakers, a number that stands at 81.

Source: NPR

4. Fatal train crash near Bedford injures 89

A train driver has died and 89 people have been injured after two trains collided in the Bedford area, with emergency services working into the night. Of those injured, 11 suffered "very serious" injuries, 22 were seriously injured, and a further 56 had minor injuries, according to the East of England ambulance service. The two East Midlands railway services involved were the 4.40pm from Corby to St Pancras and the 3.50pm from Nottingham to London St Pancras. The rail accident investigation branch said a team of inspectors was at the scene to start gathering evidence.

Why it matters: It is described as the first fatal collision between two passenger trains on a British mainline this century, with what appears in early reports to involve the highest number of serious injuries in any UK rail accident in two decades.

Source: The Guardian

5. Hormuz tanker traffic surges after US-Iran deal

At least 20 oil tankers crossed the Strait of Hormuz on Thursday, the highest level of traffic since June 2, according to trade intelligence firm Kpler. In total, 25 ships transited Hormuz on Thursday including cargo, container and other vessel classes. Iranian supertankers are switching on their transponders after going dark during the war, with five Iranian supertankers loaded with oil observed departing the region on Friday. US Vice President JD Vance told reporters Thursday that the Iranians so far "are honoring their end of the commitment".

Why it matters: After the 60-day toll-free period ends, Iran will hold talks with Oman and the Gulf states on how to administer the strait, according to the deal terms, leaving open the possibility that tolls could be imposed in the future.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

UK borrowing surges as public finances called 'fragile'

The UK borrowed £23.3bn in May, up almost a third on the same month last year, with borrowing £5.6bn higher than the Office for Budget Responsibility's forecast made in March. The Office for National Statistics said interest payable on government debt jumped to £11.7bn - the highest ever recorded in any May. Capital Economics deputy chief UK economist Ruth Gregory said: "The big picture is that the public finances are fragile," adding this would constrain whoever is Prime Minister. Separate official figures showed retail spending rose by 1.2% in May, helped by unseasonably good weather.

Source: BBC
