1. Bangkok bar fire kills at least 27

A huge fire tore through Rong Beer Na Lat Phrao, a bar in Bangkok's popular Chatuchak district, late on Sunday, killing at least 27 people and leaving eight critically injured. The blaze started near the stage and spread rapidly, cutting power and engulfing the room with smoke, with footage showing panicked customers fleeing through flame-enveloped doors. Firefighters arrived just after midnight and found the bodies of most victims in a bathroom where they had apparently sought shelter. Preliminary investigations suggest the fire could have been caused by an electrical short circuit in an air conditioner, though no official cause has been given.

Why it matters: Bangkok governor Chatchart Sittipunt said flammable interior decorations on the bar's ceiling may have facilitated the blaze's rapid spread, and there are reports of people found unconscious near the building's emergency exit, suggesting there may have been some obstruction.

Source: BBC

2. US senator Lindsey Graham dies at 71

Republican Senator Lindsey Graham, a close ally of US President Donald Trump, has died at the age of 71 following a "brief and sudden illness", according to his office. Preliminary findings from the medical examiner suggest his death was caused by a rupture of his aorta, a key artery in the heart. Graham had just returned from Kyiv, where he met Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky on Friday, and there were no known health concerns ahead of his trip. South Carolina Governor Henry McMaster now has the power to appoint a temporary replacement until the end of Graham's current term in January, with his successor to be chosen in November's midterm elections.

Why it matters: Before Graham's death, Republicans held a 53-47 majority over Democrats in the Senate, and both parties are fighting for Senate control in November.

Source: BBC

3. US-Iran strikes resume, oil jumps 4%

The US launched a new attack on Iran on Sunday evening, with Iranian state media reporting the strikes killed one person in southwestern Iran and wounded four others. Within hours, Iran's IRGC said it had struck US military bases in Kuwait, Jordan and Bahrain. The escalating attacks have cast doubt over the future of the interim US-Iran agreement signed in June, with Iran saying it has closed the Strait of Hormuz until further notice and the US insisting it is open. Brent crude jumped 4% to $79.07 a barrel on Monday morning in Asia, while US-traded oil gained 4.2% to $74.53.

Why it matters: The renewed fire has put in jeopardy an interim ceasefire agreement signed last month which aimed to reopen the strait and eventually bring a permanent end to the conflict.

Source: BBC

4. Spain wildfire toll rises to 13 dead

The death toll from Thursday's wildfire in Spain's Almeria province rose to at least 13, including five believed to be Britons, after a 93-year-old woman believed to be British died of her injuries in hospital on Sunday. The fire, which has now been contained, burned through about 7,000 hectares (17,300 acres) of land, driven by winds of up to 50 km/h (31 mph). About 600 of the nearly 1,500 people evacuated from the fire zone were told they could return on Sunday. A British couple found down a ravine, badly burned and semi-conscious and thought to have been out hiking as the blaze spread, were evacuated to hospital where they are in intensive care.

Source: BBC

5. Asian stocks slump on Middle East fears

Major Asian stock markets fell sharply on Monday amid renewed US-Iran hostilities, with South Korea's Kospi plunging over 7% to below 7,000, hitting its lowest since May 4. Japan's Nikkei 225 lost 1.57%, while Australia's S&P/ASX 200 lost 0.35% and mainland China's CSI 300 was down 0.64%. US stock futures also slipped, with Dow Jones Industrial Average futures falling 135 points (0.3%) and Nasdaq-100 futures down 0.5%. Brent crude futures rose 3.7% to $78.86 per barrel in early trading as traders weighed the Strait of Hormuz closure declaration.

Why it matters: Analyst Ben Emons wrote that "the Strait closure will hang over the market with a risk-off tone", though he noted that the focus next week will also be on CPI, Fed Chair Warsh, and bank earnings.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

June heatwave killed up to 440 a day in England

Scientists have estimated that the June heatwave killed about 440 people a day during its three-day peak in England and Wales, with about 2,700 lives lost prematurely across both the May and June heatwaves combined. More than 40% of those affected would not have died without the 1.4C of human-caused global heating to date, according to the analysis. The June heatwave was described as the widest and most intense ever recorded in Europe, estimated to have cost more than 20,000 lives continent-wide, with Germany alone seeing almost 5,500 deaths after a record high of 41.7C. Climate scientists said that climate change added 3C to 4C to the heatwave temperatures, and warned next summer may be even worse due to a major El Nino event.

Source: The Guardian

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, all UK registered companies must file accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software, with web and paper-based filing systems closed for accounts filings from that date. Small companies and micro-entities will be required to file profit and loss accounts but may opt out of publication on the public register. The implementation date has been moved from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
