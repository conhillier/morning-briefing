1. Philippines quake kills 19, triggers tsunami alerts

A magnitude-7.8 earthquake struck off the coast of Mindanao island in the southern Philippines on Monday at 07:37 local time, killing at least 19 people. At least 134 people were reportedly injured across several provinces including South Cotabato, Sultan Kudarat, and Sarangani, and the city of General Santos. The quake triggered tsunami alerts in the Philippines, Indonesia, Japan, and Australia, with some cancelled hours later. More than 130 aftershocks, with magnitudes ranging from 1.3 to 6.7, were recorded after the initial quake.

Why it matters: Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr said agencies were coordinating their disaster response, promising 'the national government is moving and we will not leave Mindanao behind', and ordered the suspension of classes in affected areas.

Source: BBC

2. Asian markets dive on Iran-Israel strikes, tech fears

Asia's major stock markets dropped sharply on Monday as the resumption of conflict between Israel and Iran combined with growing expectations of US interest rate hikes rattled investors. Chip giants Samsung Electronics dropped 10.2 percent and SK Hynix fell 7.6 percent, while Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 3.9 percent and Taiwan's TAIEX slumped 3.5 percent. Brent crude rose 3.7 percent, topping $88.50 a barrel.

Why it matters: A market analyst at IG Group told Al Jazeera the declines were triggered by 'the large correction in US tech last Friday following the blowout numbers on non-farm payrolls', with a spillover from 'fading optimism on the AI trade, particularly affecting picks-and-shovels tech companies in Asia'.

Source: Al Jazeera

3. Iran-Israel strikes rattle oil and stock markets

Tehran warned that the attacks are the start of a full week of strikes and are a response to a 'repeated violation' of the ceasefire agreed on 17 April. 4. Armenia's pro-West party wins election amid Russian pressure

Armenian Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan's Civil Contract Party secured 49.8% of the vote in Sunday's general election, with the Strong Armenia Alliance second at 23.2%. Turnout was 59%, the electoral commission said. The vote was the first general election since Armenia suffered a crushing military defeat by Azerbaijan in 2023, and was viewed as a test of Pashinyan's push to deepen ties with the West while facing mounting economic pressure from Russia. France and the EU were among European powers that congratulated Pashinyan on Monday, praising Armenia's closer ties with the West.

Why it matters: In the weeks before the election, Moscow banned the export of Armenian flowers, mineral water, cognac, fresh vegetables and fruit, and Russia supplies Armenia with gas at $177.50 per 1,000 cubic metres while European market prices exceed $600.

Source: BBC

5. Tate and Lyle agrees PS2.7bn US takeover

Tate and Lyle has agreed to a PS2.7bn takeover by US rival Ingredion, a Chicago-headquartered company employing about 11,000 people worldwide. The deal values Tate and Lyle at 615p per share, about 60% above its price before news of a possible takeover emerged. The companies warned the deal could trigger a 'material reduction' in Tate and Lyle's workforce, representing 3%, or about 475 jobs, of the new group's headcount. Tate and Lyle employs just under 5,000 people around the world, with about 200 employees in the UK, most operating from its London headquarters.

Why it matters: The takeover represents yet another loss for London's stock market, which has suffered a series of high-profile exits in recent years, including take-private deals involving Schroders, Beazley and Intertek.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Fife cattle cull proceeds without government inspectors

Scottish government inspectors will not attend the cull of 271 cattle at Falkland Estate in Fife after receiving threats online and through phone calls to their office. The Scottish government had initially said it would attend and verify the cull, but reversed the decision after people called its office trying to find the names and addresses of inspectors. The government said it had not taken the decision lightly and would verify the slaughter by means 'other than being present on site'. A source from the estate said the cull without government support would 'place a significant burden on our staff' and that 'not having that help will make this already awful process even more difficult'.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Mandatory tax adviser registration with HMRC from 18 May 2026

From 18 May 2026, a new mandatory registration requirement for paid tax advisers who interact with HMRC on behalf of clients rolls out online in stages through to 31 March 2027. Different adviser groups have staggered three-month windows to register: new advisers from 18 May, those with Self Assessment or Corporation Tax accounts from 18 August, payroll-only advisers from 18 November, and existing ASA holders from 31 December 2026. Eligible advisers who do not register by the relevant deadline will not be permitted to interact with HMRC on behalf of clients, and HMRC may apply sanctions including financial penalties if they continue to interact when told to stop.

Source: HMRC
