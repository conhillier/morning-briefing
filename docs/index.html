1. Iran Drones Kill One at Kuwait Airport

Iranian drones struck Kuwait International Airport on Wednesday, killing one person - later identified as an Indian citizen - and injuring more than 60 others, with the passenger building severely damaged. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps claimed the attack, saying it was in retaliation for US strikes on an Iranian oil tanker and Qeshm Island. Kuwait's foreign ministry ordered two Iranian diplomats to leave within 24 hours and summoned Iran's charge d'affaires.

Source: BBC

2. Ukraine Drones Strike St Petersburg Before Forum

Ukraine struck the outskirts of St Petersburg overnight, hours before the opening of the St Petersburg Economic Forum, which is due to attract thousands of guests from 130 countries. Local authorities said air defences shot down 59 drones and that three districts of St Petersburg were hit, though no one was killed. Volodymyr Zelensky confirmed Ukrainian drones had hit several locations including an oil terminal and a naval base in Kronstadt, the main outpost of Russia's Baltic Fleet. On Wednesday, seven people were also killed after a drone hit a passenger bus travelling in the Russia-controlled Donetsk region.

Why it matters: Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov said Moscow would respond to the strikes, warning that 'our responses will be systemic in nature.'

Source: BBC

3. Trump Threatens Tariffs on 60 Partners Over Labour

Donald Trump has threatened tariffs of between 10% and 12.5% on 60 trading partners - including the UK, the EU, Canada, Japan, Taiwan and China - over alleged forced labour failures. The proposed levies follow the US Supreme Court ruling in February that struck down his 'liberation day' tariffs as illegal, and are based on investigations using section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974. The EU immediately hit back, saying it expected the US to respect the tariff deal entered into last July. The UK government said it had already tackled forced labour through legislation including the Modern Slavery Act, and that no change to the UK's tariff rate was imminent.

Why it matters: The new tariffs would not take effect immediately and are subject to public comment and review, but the threat will unsettle trading partners who have fought hard to build trust with the Trump administration.

Source: The Guardian

4. Alphabet Raises $80bn for AI Infrastructure

Google's parent company Alphabet has said it plans to raise up to $80 billion in equity to fund AI infrastructure investments, described by analysts as the largest equity fundraising ever. The raise includes a $10 billion share sale to Berkshire Hathaway and a $30 billion initial raise, plus a $40 billion flexible mechanism. Alphabet said it would use the money to expand its 'world-class AI compute infrastructure to meet its unprecedented customer demand.' Alphabet shares fell as much as 4.4% after Wall Street opened following the announcement.

Why it matters: Analysts warn the huge fundraising is also a signal that meaningful returns to investors from AI infrastructure spending have so far been limited, with one strategist calling it a reminder of the 'unprecedented scale of the AI spending boom.'

Source: The Guardian

5. Iran-US Ceasefire Talks on Knife-Edge

Semiofficial Iranian news agencies reported that Iran's negotiators have stopped communicating with ceasefire mediators as tensions flared over Israel's broadening war in Lebanon. A regional official told the Associated Press that Iran had not communicated at all on Tuesday, after saying a ceasefire needed to be enforced in Lebanon for negotiations to continue. Trump called reports of a cessation in talks 'false and erroneous,' saying conversations had been going on 'including four days ago, three days ago, two days ago, one day ago and today.' A person familiar with the situation said Trump and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu had a 'tense' conversation earlier this week.

Why it matters: Lebanon has emerged as a key sticking point, with Tehran insisting any truce must also quell the fighting there, while Netanyahu wants to keep the issues separate ahead of new elections this fall.

Source: NPR

Closer to Home

Scottish Government Found in Contempt Over Salmond Files

The Scottish government has been found in contempt by the Court of Session for deliberately delaying publication of documents relating to the inquiry into former First Minister Nicola Sturgeon's handling of complaints against Alex Salmond. The court ruled that the government failed to start work on redacting the documents until after Christmas, despite being told to do so on 1 December last year. Lady Poole said the Scottish government 'deliberately failed' to carry out the redaction by the information commissioner's set date, and noted no steps had been taken to 'rectify matters and avoid future repetition.' The court admonished the government and ordered it to pay legal expenses to the information commissioner.

Source: BBC
