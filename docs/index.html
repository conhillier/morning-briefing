1. Russia's 'most massive' Kyiv attack kills 13

Russian forces launched a major drone and missile attack on Kyiv overnight, killing 13 people, in what the city's mayor described as the "most massive attack" on the Ukrainian capital. Mayor Vitaly Klitschko declared Friday a day of mourning and said around 90 people were injured, with an ambulance station among the places hit. Ukraine's air force said Russia had launched 74 missiles and 496 drones overnight, mainly targeting the capital, with 25 ballistic missiles and 12 drones striking 33 locations. Children were among the "significant number" of casualties, according to Tymur Tkachenko, head of Kyiv's military administration.

Why it matters: The attack marks the first large-scale missile and drone strike by Russia on Ukraine in more than two weeks. Ukraine's Foreign Minister called on partners to send more air defence systems, saying the country needed "not only words of condemnation but concrete action to stop Russian terror".

Source: BBC

2. EU court upholds Google's 4.1bn euro Android fine

The European Commission originally imposed a 4.3 billion euro penalty in 2018, on grounds that Google abused Android's mobile dominance by requiring manufacturers to pre-install Google Search and Chrome as a condition of access to its Play app store. A Google spokesperson said the judgement "fails to recognise" the firm's "significant investment to ensure Android remains open, interoperable and free".

Why it matters: It is the largest penalty the European Commission has ever imposed against Google.

Source: BBC

3. US-Iran Doha talks end without breakthrough

Iran and the United States concluded a round of indirect technical talks in Doha on Wednesday with no sign they had made headway towards a lasting peace. US Mideast envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner met with Qatari Emir Sheikh Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, while Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi headed the Iranian technical team; Iran's top negotiators did not attend. Two separate meetings were held: one addressing US "violations of its obligations" and a second addressing the release of $6 billion in frozen Iranian funds. Qatar said the next meeting would be scheduled as soon as possible after the conclusion of six-day funeral processions for former Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, set to begin on Saturday.

Why it matters: The Strait of Hormuz remains a key sticking point, with commercial ship movements through the strait rising by more than 50 percent in the week of June 22-28 compared with the previous week, according to data from Kpler.

Source: Al Jazeera

4. Amnesty: Sudan's RSF committed crimes against humanity in El Fasher

Amnesty International has alleged that Sudan's Rapid Support Forces committed crimes against humanity and ethnic cleansing during its campaign to capture El Fasher, the last stronghold of the Sudan armed forces in the Darfur region. The report, based on interviews with 247 people including 208 survivors, found that the RSF committed murder, torture, rape, enslavement and sexual slavery as part of a widespread and systematic attack against civilians. Amnesty concluded the RSF had also deliberately targeted children during attacks in the city in North Darfur state, and that the destruction of towns and villages between December 2024 and March 2025, including Abu Zerega, was consistent with ethnic cleansing. The report named three RSF commanders it said were responsible for serious violations of international law.

Why it matters: Amnesty called for an immediate ceasefire and the urgent deployment of an international force to protect civilians. Secretary General Agnes Callamard said: "The international community must move beyond statements of concern and take concrete steps to protect civilians, breaking the cycle of impunity."

Source: The Guardian

5. UN warns AI risks deepening global inequality

A new United Nations report from an independent international scientific panel warns that the development of artificial intelligence may exacerbate global inequality and proposes a shared framework for responsible AI development. The report notes that while more than a billion people now use AI weekly, access varies widely, "with adoption across the global south lagging far behind the global north", and that the US and China dominate in development of leading AI models as well as investment into compute infrastructure.

Why it matters: The report warns that countries relying on foreign AI models and cloud infrastructure "may gain access to AI while losing practical control over its standards, safeguards and local fit," and highlights that more than 2 billion people - almost a third of the world's population - are completely offline.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Police officer jailed 10 years for rapes on Lewis and Inverness

A Police Scotland officer, Cameron Ross, aged 39, has been jailed for 10 years after being found guilty of raping two women and subjecting a third to a campaign of abuse. Ross carried out attacks in Stornoway on the Isle of Lewis in 2012 and 2014, before abusing another woman in Inverness between 2019 and 2022. He was suspended from Police Scotland in January 2024 after the allegations came to light and resigned last month. Ross had denied the charges but was found guilty by a jury during a trial at the High Court in Edinburgh in May.

Source: BBC
