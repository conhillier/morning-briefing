1. Iran Drones Kill One, Hit Kuwait Airport

Iranian drones struck Kuwait International Airport and other buildings, killing one person and injuring more than 60. US Central Command said Iran fired two missiles at Kuwait and three at Bahrain, all of which broke apart or were intercepted, while the US launched its own strikes on Qeshm Island in the Strait of Hormuz targeting an Iranian military ground control station. Kuwait's defence ministry spokesman called the attack "criminal Iranian aggression". The attacks came as ceasefire negotiations between the US and Iran stalled.

Why it matters: Iran's foreign ministry warned that Kuwait and Bahrain bore "direct and unmistakable responsibility" for the aggression, raising the prospect of further escalation involving Gulf states hosting US bases.

Source: BBC

2. Ukraine Drones Hit St Petersburg as Forum Opens

Ukraine launched a drone strike on the outskirts of St Petersburg hours before the opening of the city's annual International Economic Forum, with President Volodymyr Zelensky confirming strikes on the city's oil terminal and a naval base in the nearby town of Kronstadt. Local authorities said air defences shot down 59 drones overnight and that three different districts of St Petersburg were hit, though no one was killed. Robert Brovdi, commander of Ukraine's unmanned systems, said the corvette Boikiy was struck.

Why it matters: The strike targeted the flagship Russian economic forum - once dubbed the "Russian Davos" - which is designed to attract foreign investment, with thousands of guests from 130 countries expected to attend.

Source: BBC

3. Drone Kills Eight on Bus in Donetsk

Eight people were killed and 10 others injured after a drone struck a passenger bus travelling through a Russia-controlled part of Ukraine, Russian state media reported. The bus was travelling between Moscow and Simferopol in Russian-occupied Crimea when it was struck in the early hours of Wednesday. The strikes came a day after Russia launched more than 700 missiles and drones overnight into Tuesday, killing at least 22 people across Ukraine.

Why it matters: Russia opened a criminal case into what it described as a "terrorist attack" on civilians, while Ukraine's government noted that Russia itself regularly attacks Ukrainian civilians with drones.

Source: BBC

4. Alphabet Raises $80bn for AI Infrastructure

Google's parent company Alphabet has said it plans to raise up to $80bn (PS59bn) in equity to fund AI infrastructure investments, described by analysts as the largest equity fundraising ever. The raise includes a $10bn share sale to Berkshire Hathaway and a $30bn initial raise, plus a $40bn flexible mechanism not earmarked for AI investment. Alphabet shares fell as much as 4.4% after Wall Street opened. The company said half of the $80bn would be used to "scale AI infrastructure and global compute", with the other $40bn covering tax obligations associated with employee equity awards.

Why it matters: Analysts warned the fundraising is also a signal that AI infrastructure spending is not yet delivering meaningful returns to investors, with one Deutsche Bank strategist noting it highlights the "unprecedented scale of the AI spending boom".

Source: The Guardian

5. UK Pays PS1.3bn for Universal Theme Park

The UK government has committed a PS1.3bn package of support to help Comcast build the Universal United Kingdom Resort near Bedford, the first Universal theme park in Europe. The package includes PS400m through the regional growth fund, a PS438m grant from the Department for Culture, Media and Sport, and an estimated PS474m from the Department for Transport on road and rail projects. Comcast has committed to investing more than PS5bn over the five-year construction period. The park is due to open in 2031 and is expected to attract about 8.5 million visitors in its first year.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Coalsnaughton Villagers Barred from Homes Eight More Weeks

Residents of 97 properties across four streets in Coalsnaughton, a former mining village in Clackmannanshire, have been told they cannot return home for at least another eight weeks while an investigation into ground movement continues. The Mining Remediation Authority (MRA) is investigating the cause of the movement, and its chief operations director Carl Banton outlined the works undertaken at a meeting on Tuesday. Clackmannanshire Council has formally asked the Scottish government for financial support, and First Minister John Swinney said he would be "very happy" to visit the area. The issue was also raised at Prime Minister's Questions by Labour MP Brian Leishman.

Source: BBC
