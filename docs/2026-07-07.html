1. Zelensky heads to NATO to demand Patriot missiles

Russia fired 351 drones and 68 missiles overnight, and all 29 ballistic missiles struck their targets, exposing a critical gap in Ukraine's defences. Zelensky blamed insufficient supplies of interceptors, saying: "As long as Patriot missiles remain in our allies' stockpiles, Russia is only encouraged to keep 'vanquishing' residential buildings." Ukraine's Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov warned that fewer Patriot interceptors are produced worldwide each month than Russia fires at Ukraine in the same period.

Why it matters: All ballistic missiles launched by Russia struck their targets, underscoring Kyiv's need for more US-made Patriot interceptor missiles - a point Zelensky will press at the NATO summit in Ankara this week.

Source: NPR

2. Russia-Ukraine war front and centre at NATO summit

NATO leaders are gathering in Turkey on Tuesday and Wednesday with the war in Ukraine high on the agenda, more than four years after Russia launched its full-scale invasion. Ukraine struck the main oil refinery in St Petersburg for the third time this summer, and also attacked Russia's largest oil refinery in Omsk in Siberia, 1,700 miles from the front line, while a total power blackout hit Crimea from drone strikes. These Ukrainian strikes on Russian energy infrastructure have caused what NPR's Charles Maynes described as "a massive shortage of gas at the pump" across Russia, with some regions "bone dry" and waits of two to three hours or more elsewhere. Zelensky called President Trump on July 4 to congratulate the US on its 250th birthday, and said he still hopes the US will engage in peace talks.

Why it matters: Ukraine's long-range drone campaign is bringing the war home to Russians and has changed minds in Europe and Washington, with Trump recently acknowledging he is impressed by Ukraine's fighting spirit.

Source: NPR

3. Sky buys ITV broadcast arm for £1.6bn

The deal would mark the end of 70 years of ITV as an independent public service broadcaster, though it faces 12 to 18 months of regulatory scrutiny. Sky has committed to spending at least £2.1bn between 2028 and 2032 on ITV Studios as part of a long-term partnership, and Sky's chief executive Dana Strong pledged that shows such as Coronation Street, Love Island and I'm a Celebrity would not go behind a paywall.

Why it matters: Sky has identified approximately £200m in annual cost synergies to be realised by the end of the third year after the deal closes, with a "minority" of savings coming from job cuts where there is duplication, primarily in corporate and commercial functions.

Source: The Guardian

4. EasyJet agrees £5.5bn Castlelake takeover

EasyJet's board has agreed to recommend a £5.5bn takeover by US private equity firm Castlelake at £6.90 a share, after rejecting four previous bids as low as £5.60 per share. Castlelake must table a formal bid by 3 August under City takeover rules, and signalled it would back easyJet's current strategy and was not looking to break up the company. EasyJet employs 19,000 people and flies about 93 million passengers a year, though its shares had fallen by about 30% in the past year before Castlelake's bid interest emerged.

Why it matters: Garry White of Charles Stanley said the deal confirmed that "many UK-listed companies remain significantly undervalued" and that "UK companies are currently being sold off on the cheap."

Source: The Guardian

5. Samsung profits surge 1,800% on AI chip demand

Samsung Electronics has forecast a 19-fold jump in profits, with expected operating profits of 89.4tn won (£43.6bn; $58.4bn) for the April-to-June quarter - its third record quarterly result in a row. The company also reported around 171tn won of sales during the quarter, more than double the amount for the same period last year, driven by surging demand for AI memory chips.

Why it matters: Research firm IDC warned that demand for semiconductors for data centres and other AI infrastructure has been "different from anything the memory industry has navigated", and that supplies are expected to remain tight through next year.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Scottish AI datacentre's renewables pledge 'undeliverable'

A Guardian investigation has found that a landmark £8.2bn AI datacentre complex in Lanarkshire, built by US firm CoreWeave and Scottish company DataVita, has no prospect of meeting its promise to be powered entirely from on-site renewables by 2030. The government has since said the complex will connect to the national grid, meaning it will either join a years-long queue or be expedited ahead of hundreds of other projects.

Source: The Guardian

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, small companies and micro-entities must file profit and loss accounts with Companies House but may opt out of publication on the public register. All UK registered companies will be required to file accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software only, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings from that date. Abridged accounts will no longer be permitted, and a strengthened eligibility statement will be required for companies claiming an audit exemption.

Source: Companies House
