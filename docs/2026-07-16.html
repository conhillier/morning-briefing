1. US launches fresh strikes on Iran Wednesday

The US said it had launched a fresh wave of strikes against Iran on Wednesday as President Donald Trump warned Tehran it "better behave". The US military said "Iranian military capabilities used to threaten vessels" moving through the Strait of Hormuz had been targeted, with a 90-minute wave hitting coastal defences and cruise missile storage and launch sites on Greater Tunb Island. Iran said it had struck US military targets in the region, including in Bahrain and Kuwait, as a fifth day of renewed hostilities strained their preliminary deal to end the war. Iran's top negotiator, Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf, told state media Tehran had "no reason" to abide by the deal if it did not benefit from it.

Why it matters: Ongoing US-Iran hostilities have underscored the strategic importance of the Strait to the global economy, with a sharp rise in oil prices triggered by tanker traffic virtually stalling through the key shipping lane.

Source: BBC

2. US imposes 25% tariffs on Brazilian goods

The US has levied 25% tariffs on most imports from Brazil effective next week, concluding a yearlong investigation into what Washington calls unfair trade practices. The action, taken under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974, targets Brazilian practices including orders directing American technology firms - including X, Meta, and Google - to remove certain political content, preferential tariffs for Mexico and India, weak intellectual property enforcement, and ethanol market barriers. The 25% levy, set to take effect on July 22, will apply to most imports from Brazil, with exemptions for certain goods such as beef, orange juice, aircraft and parts, and energy products. A separate US probe into forced-labor enforcement could see an additional 12.5% duty on Brazilian goods on top of the 25%, with a decision due next week.

Why it matters: The dispute has also spilled into Brazil's upcoming presidential election in October, with Lula accusing Senator Flavio Bolsonaro of helping trigger the tariffs after his Washington visit.

Source: CNBC

3. China Q2 growth slowest since 2022

Monthly car exports topped 1 million for the first time in June, but domestic vehicle sales plummeted by more than 16%, highlighting how dependent China's economy has become on selling goods abroad. Fixed-asset investment - including spending on bridges, roads and infrastructure - declined by more than 4% between January and May, a contraction that has happened only twice since the founding of the People's Republic of China, in 1961 and 1967. Overall growth in the economy for the first half of the year was 4.7%, within Beijing's target range, which may reduce pressure on policymakers for large-scale intervention.

Why it matters: The global economy is also under strain from the US-Israel war on Iran, which risks reducing global demand for Chinese goods, and a global recession would cause long-term pain for the export-driven Chinese economy.

Source: The Guardian

4. Mahmood frontrunner for Burnham chancellor role

Shabana Mahmood has emerged as the frontrunner to become Andy Burnham's chancellor after a fierce briefing war over the prospect of Ed Miliband being appointed to the powerful role. Senior Labour figures with knowledge of Burnham's thinking told the Guardian they expected the home secretary to be moved to the Treasury amid concerns that Miliband would become a target for criticism of the government. The pound rallied and UK government borrowing costs fell slightly on Wednesday after reports that Mahmood was the favourite to enter No 11, with the yield on a 10-year bond dropping 0.06 percentage points during the day. Burnham's team insisted decisions over the makeup of the cabinet were mostly speculation, adding that his cabinet would be appointed only after he enters No 10 on Monday.

Why it matters: Mahmood's first big task, if confirmed as chancellor on Monday, would be to draw up an emergency cost of living package that could include targeted support for energy bills, a rent freeze and a cap on bus fares.

Source: The Guardian

5. China-North Korea ties deepen in Pyongyang talks

China's fourth-highest-ranked official, Wang Huning, held talks in Pyongyang with a top official from North Korea's governing Workers' Party, Jo Yong Won, according to the state-run Korean Central News Agency. Wang affirmed "the will of the Chinese party and the government" to implement the agreement reached between Xi Jinping and North Korean leader Kim Jong Un during Xi's visit to Pyongyang last month - the first such visit in seven years. During last month's summit, the two leaders adopted what North Korean state media described as a "far-reaching blueprint" for strengthening "the most powerful and strategic relations", with Xi pushing for closer diplomatic, law enforcement and military cooperation. The two officials also discussed ways to improve public welfare and deepen mutual cooperation in business and culture.

Source: Al Jazeera

Closer to Home

Cairngorms wildfire forces Highland evacuations

Villagers, campsites and ski resorts have been evacuated as a "large-scale" wildfire spread across 2km of dry land in the Cairngorms National Park, with fire crews battling flames since about 11:50 after they took hold at Ryvoan Bothy at Nethy Bridge. The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service said there were no reported casualties, but members of the public are being urged to avoid Glenmore Forest Park and Loch Morlich into Thursday. Deputy assistant chief officer Ian McMeekin said warm, high winds had caused the fire to spread across areas of dry land, and that crews anticipated being on scene for a considerable time. The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service and the Scottish Wildlife Forum issued a wildfire warning for central Highlands, southern and eastern Scotland lasting until next Monday, with temperatures expected to reach 28C around Fort William.

Source: BBC
