1. US launches 11th night of Iran strikes

The US launched strikes on Iran for the 11th consecutive night late on Tuesday, with US Central Command saying the wave of strikes were "designed to continue degrading Iran's ability to threaten commercial shipping" in the Strait of Hormuz. Explosions were reported in Tabriz, Chabahar, Konarak, and in Bushehr, home to Iran's only nuclear power plant.

Why it matters: Iran's Khatam Al-Anbiya military command warned it would consider an attack on the nuclear site "as an expansion of war in the region", threatening strikes on "all the interests of America, the allies and supporters". Kuwait's army said early on Wednesday its air defences were engaging with drone attacks from Iran, while Bahrain and Jordan said they had also intercepted drone and missile strikes.

Source: BBC

2. Houthis threaten Red Sea shipping embargo

A Houthi media official said they were closing the Bab al-Mandab Strait - the Red Sea's southern gateway - to Saudi vessels. Following the announcement, two tankers which had just loaded Saudi crude bound for China and India at Saudi Arabia's Red Sea port of Yanbu made U-turns on Tuesday, heading instead towards the Suez Canal, according to British maritime risk management group Vanguard. Trump also threatened to "take care of" the Houthi rebels if the Iran-backed militant group followed through on its threatened blockade of Saudi ports. The Red Sea has become crucial for oil shipments from Saudi Arabia during the US-Israeli war with Iran, which has led to the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Why it matters: The threat from the Houthis risks further disruption to international shipping, with hostilities in the region having already led to global fuel prices fluctuating significantly.

Source: BBC

3. Zelensky sacks Ukraine army commander-in-chief

Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelensky has dismissed the country's army commander-in-chief, Oleksandr Syrskyi, just days after the removal of popular Defence Minister Mykhailo Fedorov triggered street protests across the country. Syrskyi, 60, was replaced by Mykhailo Drapatyi, 43, described as a popular, battle-hardened general. Fedorov congratulated Drapatyi, describing his appointment as a "new hope in the fight of free people for freedom and justice" and saying it was "the voice of change that could not be ignored". Zelensky has asked acting Defence Minister Khmara and Drapatyi to come up with a new defence strategy covering key issues like reforming the military corps system, weapons supply and drones.

Why it matters: Fedorov's clear endorsement of Drapatyi is likely to go some way towards defusing the political crisis of the past week, though protesters will still want to see Fedorov back in a position of real authority.

Source: BBC

4. TSMC US expansion squeezes margins

Pressure from President Donald Trump to manufacture advanced semiconductors in the US is increasing costs and squeezing margins at TSMC, the world's leading chipmaker. Since Trump's return to power in 2025, TSMC has announced a total of $200 billion in commitments to the US, including last week's unveiling of a $100 billion investment into advanced semiconductor manufacturing and packaging facilities. TSMC reported a 77.4% jump in second-quarter profit year on year, but gross margin was offset by dilution from overseas fabs, CFO Wendell Huang said. The company forecasts gross margin dilution from the ramp-up of overseas fabs to be 2% to 3% in the early stages, widening to 3% to 4% in the latter stages.

Why it matters: Analysts estimate TSMC's US chips cost 20-50% more than those produced in Taiwan, and because of TSMC's dominance in the leading-edge node market, "a large part of the increased costs would have to be absorbed by its clients," according to Gartner VP analyst Gaurav Gupta.

Source: CNBC

5. Burnham's cost of living push faces funding questions

On his first full day in power, Prime Minister Andy Burnham urged his cabinet to examine "all possible ways" to tackle the cost of living crisis, telling ministers his would be a "cost of living government". Sources say measures will include reducing the national bus fare cap by a third, from £3 to £2, with an announcement expected on Wednesday. Burnham began with an announcement that VAT would be removed from electricity bills from 1 October, reducing the electricity price cap by £45 at a total cost of £850m next year.

Why it matters: Downing Street said the tax cut was funded for the next financial year, but money would have to be found in future years from existing departmental budgets, suggesting spending cuts to come.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Sarwar to quit Holyrood for Lords ministerial role

Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar is set to stand down to become a minister in Andy Burnham's government, leaving the Scottish Parliament and his leader role to take up a seat in the House of Lords. It is thought Sarwar will become a business minister. Sarwar publicly called for Sir Keir Starmer to resign in February, and Scottish Labour won just 17 seats out of 129 in the Scottish Parliament elections in May. His appointment is expected to create a leadership vacancy, though there would be no by-election for his Holyrood seat as he was elected via the regional list.

Source: BBC
