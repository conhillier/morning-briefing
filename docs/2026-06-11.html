1. US and Iran exchange strikes for second day

The US and Iran have exchanged strikes across the Middle East for a second consecutive day, further straining an already shaky ceasefire agreed between the two countries in April. US Central Command said it had completed a wave of "self-defense strikes" targeting military, surveillance and radar sites in southern Iran, while Iran's IRGC said it fired 12 ballistic missiles at the Muwaffaq Salti Airbase in Jordan, claiming to have destroyed "a large number" of US fighter jets and facilities. US military bases in Bahrain and Kuwait came under Iranian fire for a second day in a row, and Kuwait said it had temporarily closed its airspace due to the Iranian attacks. The IRGC also said it had hit two oil tankers passing through the Strait of Hormuz, with Iranian state media reporting the strait was "completely closed to all type of vessel", though US Central Command said commercial ships were continuing to transit.

Why it matters: UN Secretary General Antonio Guterres warned the Middle East was "being pulled deeper into crisis", saying "the ceasefire is more like a lesser-fire" and calling on all parties to work towards a diplomatic settlement.

Source: BBC

2. Markets price in prolonged Iran war 'long grind'

Investors are increasingly grappling with the possibility of a prolonged US-Iran conflict and pricing in a "long grind", according to market strategists. Oil last traded up about 2% on Thursday but has stayed below $100 a barrel, as traders still see enough buffers in the market to prevent a full-blown supply shock. Fitch Ratings this week downgraded its global sovereign sector outlook to "deteriorating" from "neutral", citing the impact of the US-Iran war, with the agency expecting the conflict to weaken global growth, raise inflation and bond yields, and increase geopolitical risks. Billy Leung of Global X ETFs said: "With mediation collapsing and strikes resuming, markets have moved from pricing a ceasefire to pricing a long grind."

Why it matters: Leung warned that "a prolonged war ends the era of buying everything and being rewarded", adding that with energy costs and the real cost of capital both rising, "earnings hurdles move higher across the board."

Source: CNBC

3. Oracle stock drops despite earnings beat

Oracle reported better-than-expected earnings and revenue for its fiscal fourth quarter, with adjusted earnings per share of $2.03 against an expected $1.96, and revenue of $19.18 billion versus the $19.10 billion consensus. Despite beating forecasts and lifting its adjusted earnings per share forecast for the year to $8.05, the stock dropped 10% in extended trading after the company announced plans to raise an additional $20 billion in equity and debt on top of what has already been announced. For the fiscal year, Oracle reported $23.7 billion in negative free cash flow, with capital expenditures jumping 162% to $55.7 billion. The company's remaining performance obligation reached $638 billion on May 31, up 363%, with Bank of America analysts noting that over 50% of that figure comes from OpenAI.

Source: CNBC

4. Korea fines Coupang record $400m over data breach

Seoul's Personal Information Protection Commission (PIPC) announced a fine of 624.68bn won on Coupang "for violating safety obligations and collecting personal data without legal grounds", finding that poor management of authentication signing keys and access controls had resulted in the personal data of around 37.5 million users being exposed. The number of accounts affected represents more than half of South Korea's population of around 50 million people. Coupang told the BBC it "deeply regrets the concern caused" and plans to strengthen its security measures, but said it would challenge the PIPC decision.

Why it matters: The fine is the largest ever issued by Seoul's Personal Information Protection Commission for a data breach, coming after a months-long probe following allegations of the data leak surfacing in November.

Source: BBC

5. BYD targets world's top automaker spot within five years

Chinese electric vehicle maker BYD has said it aims to be the world's biggest automaker within the next five years, targeting Toyota's long-held top spot. The company also announced plans to spend nearly £1.8bn in Europe to develop infrastructure for five-minute "flash charging" of its cars.

Why it matters: BYD is also facing pressure in the US after the Pentagon overnight added it to a list of "Chinese military companies" deemed a national security risk, with China responding that its addition to the list "lacks factual basis".

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Scottish councils face £530m budget shortfall

Scotland's councils are facing a collective budget shortfall of about £530m in 2026-27, with the Accounts Commission warning that local authorities will have to "stop, reduce, or significantly redesign" services. The watchdog found that a small 2% rise in revenue funding was not enough to keep pace with rising costs and demand, particularly on social care, with councils forecast to spend £4.3bn on social care over the next year - almost a quarter of their revenue spending. Budget gaps range from £86.7m in Glasgow City to £3.5m in East Lothian, with South Lanarkshire the only council projecting a surplus of £1.5m. Every local authority in Scotland has increased council tax to cope - ranging from 4% in Edinburgh to 10% in Aberdeenshire and Moray - expected to raise about £248m in total.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, small companies and micro-entities must file profit and loss accounts with Companies House but may opt out of having that information published on the public register. All UK registered companies will be required to file accounts in iXBRL format using commercial software, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings from that date. The implementation date has moved from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
