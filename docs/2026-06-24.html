1. Congress rebukes Trump on Iran war

The Republican-controlled US Senate approved a measure instructing President Donald Trump to halt the war in Iran or seek congressional approval before continuing military action, in a 50-48 vote on Tuesday. The vote marks the first time that both chambers of Congress have approved a concurrent resolution instructing a president to end a military action since the War Powers Resolution of 1973 was enacted. Trump criticised the resolution, writing on Truth Social: "So, I have Iran on the 'ropes,' ready to go down for the fall... and the U.S. Senate decides to have a poorly timed and meaningless War Powers Act Vote." Middle East analyst Laura Blumenfeld called it "more of a slap on a wrist than a handcuff, because it has no legal binding."

Why it matters: The resolution adds to pressure on the White House to find an end to the Iran war, which is unpopular with the public after petrol prices spiked, though it carries no legal force.

Source: BBC

2. UN to evacuate 11,000 stranded Hormuz sailors

The UN's International Maritime Organization (IMO) is set to evacuate more than 11,000 sailors who have been stranded in the Gulf because of the US-Israel war against Iran. IMO secretary-general Arsenio Dominguez said the "large-scale operation" would be carried out in cooperation with Iran, Oman, the US, other coastal states in the region and the maritime industry. US Secretary of State Marco Rubio, beginning a tour of the Gulf on Tuesday, warned that no country is allowed to impose tolls on the Strait of Hormuz, stating: "It's an international waterway. No country is allowed to charge tolls or fees on an international waterway.

Why it matters: Evacuating stranded sailors hinges on the Strait remaining open, and conflicting signals from Iranian officials about the strait's status continue to complicate the picture.

Source: BBC

3. Only a trickle of ships through Hormuz

At least 172 vessels have crossed through the Strait of Hormuz since the US and Iran signed a deal, including 42 ships on Saturday alone, according to maritime intelligence firm Kpler, but this remains well below the pre-conflict average of some 138 crossings each day. More than 250 tankers and 440 cargo ships are still inside the Gulf based on their last reported positions, with more than 80% of tankers stationary or at anchor. The Joint Maritime Information Center has warned ships to avoid the central part of the strait "due to the existence of mines", and has so far issued warnings for two mines with active clearance operations ongoing. Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps said on Saturday the strait had been closed in response to Israeli strikes on Lebanon, though some traffic continued to flow.

Why it matters: The price of a barrel of Brent crude has dropped to its lowest level since the war began, but ongoing uncertainty over mines, Iranian permits and contradictory official statements is keeping ship traffic well below normal levels.

Source: BBC

4. Europe heatwave kills dozens, records shattered

Forty people have drowned in heatwave-related deaths in France since Thursday, Prime Minister Sebastien Lecornu said, as France saw its hottest June day on record on Tuesday, reaching an average of 29.8C. In Spain, temperatures are set to peak above 40C in some areas, with red alerts in Andalusia, Cantabria and the Basque Country, while in Italy a red heatwave alert has been declared in 15 cities including Rome, Milan, Florence, Turin and Venice. Two children aged two and four were found dead in their family car in a car park in the southern French city of Carpentras on Monday. The Eiffel Tower closed early on Tuesday because of the high temperatures, shutting at 16:00 rather than its usual 00:45.

Why it matters: Meteo France said the red alert in much of France would be extended on Wednesday from 54 to 58 of the country's 96 metropolitan departments, and the president of the Ile-de-France region warned of major public transport disruption as rail tracks cannot withstand temperatures above 50C.

Source: BBC

5. Murrell jailed five years for SNP embezzlement

Former SNP chief executive Peter Murrell has been jailed for embezzling more than £400,000 in party funds over a 12-year period, sentenced to five years and three months on Tuesday. New images released by the Crown Office and Procurator Fiscal Service show items he purchased using stolen money, including a £124,550 Niessman and Bischoff motorhome that was driven to his mother's address in Dunfermline and seized by police on 5 April 2023. Murrell made many repeat purchases of the same items, including nine vacuum cleaners costing just under £4,000 bought in the space of four years, along with Lalique salt and pepper grinders bought in 2020 for £2,618. His estranged wife and former First Minister Nicola Sturgeon told the BBC's Laura Kuenssberg she had no "conscious memory" of ever seeing the luxury motorhome.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

UK braces for record 40C heat Wednesday

The UK is braced for record-breaking temperatures, with schools closing and people urged not to travel, as temperatures could hit 40C on Wednesday and Thursday in some parts of England and Wales. A red weather warning for extreme heat is in place covering an area stretching from London to Swansea and Somerset to Birmingham. Italy's health ministry has declared a red heatwave alert in 16 cities for Wednesday including Milan and Rome, as the extreme weather spreads slowly eastwards. The extreme weather is being driven by atmospheric and circulation patterns that keep hot air trapped in place for days, exacerbated by global heating, experts say.

Source: The Guardian
