1. Israel and Iran trade fresh strikes, ceasefire falters

Iran's IRGC said early Monday it launched attacks on Israel's Nevatim and Tel Nof airbases in response to Israeli overnight strikes on radar sites across Iran, with Israel's army then detecting a new barrage of missiles from Iran. Israeli forces had earlier struck targets at the petrochemical complex in Mahshahr in southwestern Iran, after hitting military targets elsewhere. Iranian media reported explosions in Tehran, Tabriz and Isfahan, while Yemen's Houthis also fired a salvo at Israel and announced a ban on Israeli maritime navigation in the Red Sea. 2. Asian markets tumble on tech fears and Middle East strikes

South Korea's Kospi plunged more than 8% on Monday, triggering a circuit breaker for the third time this year, while Japan's Nikkei 225 dropped 3.85% to 64,024.6. The sell-off extended a sharp drop on Wall Street on Friday, where the Nasdaq fell 4.18% to 25,709.43 - its biggest drop since April 2025.

Why it matters: Investors are seeking clear signs that AI demand has translated into real revenue, and the public debut of SpaceX on Friday is expected to be one of the largest in Wall Street history, representing a major test of the AI valuation narrative.

Source: CNBC

3. Zelensky allies set five conditions for Ukraine peace

Ukraine and its close European allies - the UK, France and Germany - set out five conditions for a "just and lasting" deal to end the war with Russia, following talks at 10 Downing Street in London on Sunday. The conditions include a stop to the fighting, starting negotiations from the current position in the field, and "robust" security guarantees for Ukraine. The leaders also reiterated the need for the US to be part of the process. They supported Zelensky's call for "a direct dialogue between Ukraine and Russia - with active US and European participation".

Why it matters: The leaders said Ukraine's right "to choose its own security arrangements and alliances must be fully respected", in an apparent reference to Moscow's insistence that Ukraine give up its ambition to join NATO.

Source: BBC

4. Russia drone hits Chornobyl nuclear fuel storage

A Russian drone struck a storage facility for spent nuclear fuel near the Chornobyl nuclear plant, according to Kyiv officials. Ukraine's state-owned nuclear operator Enerhoatom said the strike "partially destroyed" the spent nuclear fuel storage building, though there were no injuries, a fire was put out, and radiation remained at a normal level.

Why it matters: Enerhoatom criticised Moscow for what it called a deliberate threat to nuclear safety.

Source: BBC

5. Tata Steel Port Talbot furnace faces eight-month delay

Plans for a £1.25bn electric arc furnace at Port Talbot may be delayed by up to eight months due to problems with electrical connectivity, Tata Steel has warned. Tata boss Koushik Chatterjee warned investors during a conference call last month that National Grid has formally alerted the company that their connectivity project is delayed, which is critical for the project's commissioning. The furnace was hoped to be up and running by the end of 2027, replacing traditional blast furnaces that closed two years ago with the loss of 2,000 jobs. National Grid said the work involved constructing two new substations, installing transformers and laying 2km of underground cables, with issues including ground conditions and environmental and planning considerations contributing to delays.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Fife cattle cull to proceed without Scottish inspectors

Scottish government inspectors will not attend the slaughter of 271 cattle at Falkland Estate in Fife after receiving threats online and through phone calls to their office. The estate made the decision to slaughter the animals after its farm failed to register them properly, meaning the cows could not enter the food chain. The government said it would verify the slaughter by means "other than being present on site". A source from the estate told the BBC: "Not having that help will make this already awful process even more difficult."

Source: BBC
