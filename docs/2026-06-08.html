1. Israel and Iran trade strikes, war risk rises

Israel launched strikes on central and western Iran early Monday, hitting a petrochemical factory in Mahshahr in Khuzestan province, after Iran fired two waves of ballistic missiles at Israel. The escalation marks the most serious crossfire since an April 8 ceasefire was reached in the Iran war, which was launched on February 28 when Israel and the United States killed Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei. Sirens also sounded in Saudi Arabia near Prince Sultan Air Base, and a missile launched from Yemen targeted Israel, though Israel's rescue services reported no casualties. Trump told the Financial Times: 'I call the shots. I call all the shots. He (Netanyahu) doesn't call the shots.'

Why it matters: With Iran still holding a vast stockpile of highly enriched uranium and Yemen's Houthi rebels apparently getting involved in the fighting Monday, the risks of the war fully erupting again appears to be rising.

Source: NPR

2. Asia tech stocks extend sell-off on AI fears

Asian tech stocks fell sharply on Monday, extending a sell-off after the US Nasdaq declined more than 4.5% last week. Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix fell 5% and 2% respectively, while South Korea's Kospi plunged as much as 8%. SoftBank Group plunged 7.5% and TSMC was down 2.1%. The sell-off was triggered after Broadcom's revenue for fiscal second quarter missed market estimates, with the VanEck Semiconductor ETF losing over 9% on Friday. According to a UOB note, 'The tech-led rout erased approximately $1.8 trillion in S&P 500 market cap.'

Why it matters: The sell-off follows a recent rally in Asia tech stocks supported by investor optimism on AI demand, during which Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix each crossed a $1 trillion market valuation.

Source: CNBC

3. Zelensky allies set five conditions for peace

Ukraine and its close European allies set out five conditions for a 'just and lasting' deal to end the war with Russia, following talks at 10 Downing Street in London on Sunday. In a joint statement, Zelensky, UK Prime Minister Sir Keir Starmer, French President Emmanuel Macron and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said they would 'stand firmly with Ukraine'. The conditions include a stop to the fighting, starting negotiations from the current battlefield position, and 'robust' security guarantees for Ukraine. The leaders also reiterated the need for the US to be part of the process.

Why it matters: The leaders welcomed Zelensky's letter to Putin and supported his call for 'a direct dialogue between Ukraine and Russia - with active US and European participation'.

Source: BBC

4. Russia strikes Chornobyl nuclear fuel storage

A Russian drone struck a storage facility for spent nuclear fuel near the Chornobyl nuclear plant, according to Kyiv officials, partially destroying the building. Ukraine's state-owned nuclear operator Enerhoatom said there were no injuries, a fire was put out, and radiation remained at a normal level. Zelensky described the attack as 'vile', saying 'Russia deliberately struck this particular nuclear infrastructure facility'. At least three people were also killed in a separate Russian strike in a village outside Zaporizhzhia.

Why it matters: Enerhoatom criticised Moscow for what it called a deliberate threat to nuclear safety.

Source: BBC

5. Tata Steel furnace faces up to eight-month delay

Plans for a £1.25bn electric arc furnace at Port Talbot may be delayed by up to eight months due to problems with electrical connectivity, Tata Steel has warned. Tata boss Koushik Chatterjee told investors last month that National Grid has formally alerted the company that its connectivity project is delayed, which is 'critical for Tata Steel UK for the project commissioning'. The furnace had been expected to be operational by the end of 2027, replacing traditional blast furnaces that closed two years ago with the loss of 2,000 jobs. National Grid said the work involves constructing two new substations, installing transformers, and laying 2km of underground cables.

Why it matters: Tata Steel said it is discussing 'potential adjustments to the commissioning timetable' with partners including the UK government and National Grid to deliver the project 'safely and as quickly as possible'.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Fife cattle cull to proceed without inspectors

Scottish government inspectors will not attend the slaughter of 271 cattle at Falkland Estate in Fife after receiving threats online and through phone calls. The estate decided to cull the herd after Home Farm failed to register its Aberdeen Angus and Belted Galloway cattle under disease prevention rules, meaning the animals could not enter the food chain. The government said it had received 'attempts to find out the names and addresses of the inspectors via calls to the office and threats to our inspectors on social media', and will verify the slaughter by means 'other than being present on site'. A source from the estate said the situation would 'place a significant burden on our staff'.

Source: BBC
