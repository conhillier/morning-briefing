1. Philippines quake kills 19, triggers tsunami alerts

A magnitude-7.8 earthquake struck off the coast of Mindanao island in the southern Philippines on Monday at 07:37 local time, killing at least 19 people. At least 134 people were reportedly injured across several provinces - South Cotabato, Sultan Kudarat, Sarangani - and the city of General Santos. More than 130 aftershocks, with magnitudes ranging from 1.3 to 6.7, were recorded after the initial quake. The quake triggered tsunami alerts in the Philippines, Indonesia, Japan and Australia, with a 1.4m wave measured at some coastal locations.

Why it matters: Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr said agencies were coordinating their disaster response, promising 'the national government is moving and we will not leave Mindanao behind.'

Source: BBC

2. South Korea market halted in Asia tech rout

South Korea's Kospi index plunged nearly 9% within minutes of opening on Monday, forcing the exchange to halt trading for 20 minutes under its circuit breaker mechanism; it eventually closed down 8.3%. Chipmakers Samsung fell 10% and SK Hynix also suffered heavy losses.

Why it matters: Investors are looking for clear signs that AI demand has translated into 'real revenue', with chief investment strategist Charu Chanana from Saxo saying 'the burden of proof has gone up.'

Source: BBC

3. Iran-Israel strikes rattle oil markets

Tehran warned that the attacks are the start of a full week of strikes and described them as a response to a 'repeated violation' of a ceasefire agreed on 17 April. Israel hit back with attacks on military targets in Iran, despite US President Donald Trump urging the country not to retaliate. Trump told news outlet Axios: 'We are very close to a final deal with Iran. It is going to be a good deal. I don't want it to blow up because of what is happening now.'

Why it matters: Oil prices are expected to be volatile unless diplomatic efforts succeed, according to Associate Professor Jiajia Yang from James Cook University, with the war having disrupted the flow of oil and gas shipments from the Gulf.

Source: BBC

4. Armenia's pro-West party wins election

Armenian Prime Minister Nikol Pashinyan's Civil Contract Party secured 49.8% of the vote in Sunday's general election, with the pro-Russian Strong Armenia Alliance coming second with 23.2%. The result came despite mounting economic pressure from Russia, including a ban in the two weeks preceding the election on the export of Armenian flowers, mineral water, cognac, fresh vegetables and fruit. Pashinyan declared victory on Monday, saying 'Armenian people voted for peace, regional prosperity and cooperation.' France and the EU were among European powers that congratulated Pashinyan on Monday, praising Armenia's closer ties with the West.

Why it matters: Civil Contract's success came despite Pashinyan's domestic support falling from 54% in 2021 to around 30% today, according to polls, reflecting the divisive legacy of the 2023 loss of Nagorno-Karabakh.

Source: BBC

5. Ukraine and allies set five peace conditions

The leaders of Ukraine, the UK, France and Germany set out five conditions for a 'just and lasting' deal to end the war with Russia after talks in London on Sunday. The conditions include a stop to the fighting, starting negotiations from the current position in the field, and 'robust' security guarantees for Ukraine. Before arriving in London, Zelensky accused Russia of a 'vile' attack after a drone struck a storage facility for spent nuclear fuel near the Chornobyl nuclear plant, though Ukraine's state nuclear operator said there were no injuries and radiation remained at a normal level.

Why it matters: The leaders said Ukraine's right 'to choose its own security arrangements and alliances must be fully respected', an apparent challenge to Moscow's insistence that Ukraine abandon its ambition to join NATO.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

Threats halt inspectors at Fife cattle cull

Scottish government inspectors will not attend the cull of 271 cattle at Falkland Estate in Fife after people called its office trying to find the names and addresses of inspectors and made threats on social media. The government said it would be verifying the slaughter by means 'other than being present on site.' The estate made the decision to slaughter the animals after its farm failed to register them properly, meaning the cows - Aberdeen Angus and Belted Galloway - could not enter the food chain. A source from the estate told the BBC that not having government support would 'place a significant burden on our staff' and would make 'this already awful process even more difficult.'

Source: BBC
