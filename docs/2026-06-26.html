1. Venezuela quake death toll hits 235

Rescuers are searching through rubble after two powerful earthquakes struck Venezuela near the capital, killing at least 235 people and injuring at least 4,300. The first 7.2-magnitude quake was followed seconds later by an even stronger 7.5-magnitude one, according to the US Geological Survey, with both occurring close to the surface, making destruction more severe. The USGS estimated there was a 42% chance of more than 10,000 deaths and a 33% chance of more than 100,000 as a result of the earthquakes. The second earthquake is the strongest to hit Venezuela since 1900, USGS records show.

Why it matters: With at least 30 aftershocks recorded, Venezuela's main international airport closed, and power cuts hampering rescue assessments, the USGS warned that 'high casualties and extensive damage are probable and the disaster is likely widespread.'

Source: BBC

2. Hormuz evacuation paused after ship attacked

The UN's International Maritime Organization has paused the planned evacuation of more than 11,000 sailors stranded in the Strait of Hormuz after a cargo ship was attacked. The British maritime security agency UKMTO reported that the Singapore-flagged Ever Lovely was struck 7.5 nautical miles southeast of Oman's port of Dahit by 'an unknown projectile', with no casualties reported. US officials said Iran had fired on the ship, according to US media reports, and the Persian Gulf Strait Authority said vessels using unauthorised routes would not be guaranteed safe passage. IMO chief Arsenio Dominguez said the vessel that was attacked 'did not transit under IMO's evacuation framework' and that the plan would be paused 'until further clarity is obtained.'

Why it matters: The pause threatens the coordinated effort to free thousands of sailors stranded since February, as Iran's body managing the strait warned that 'any consequences arising from the use of unauthorised routes shall be the responsibility of the vessel's owner, operator and master.'

Source: BBC

3. Apple and Xbox hit consumers with steep price hikes

Apple has increased prices across its range of Macs and iPads, with the base model MacBook Air rising from $1,099 to $1,299 and the Mac Studio M3 Ultra jumping from $3,999 to $5,299, its steepest rise. The company blamed an 'extraordinary surge' in demand for memory and storage chips to power AI data centres, saying 'we have never seen a component price increase this much, this quickly.' Apple's stock price fell more than 6 percent following the announcement, its steepest fall since Trump's 'liberation day' tariffs.

Why it matters: Microsoft warned that console storage and memory prices have already increased by more than 2.5x and it expects another doubling by the fall of 2027, leaving the door open for further price rises across consumer electronics.

Source: Al Jazeera

4. Paris bans alcohol as heatwave shifts east

French authorities have announced public alcohol consumption and sales bans in Paris, with Parisians restricted from drinking alcohol in public from noon on Friday until 07:00 on Saturday, and again during the same hours over the weekend. Paris police chief Patrice Faure said: 'We are reaching a saturation point in hospital facilities,' while Paris mayor Emmanuel Gregoire warned that the mortality rate was on the rise in the capital. The ambulance service in Paris had seen four times more cardiac arrests than normal over a 24-hour period. Heatwave conditions are now set to shift east, with temperatures in Germany potentially hitting 40C on Friday and an extreme weather warning in place across much of the Czech Republic.

Why it matters: French Prime Minister Sebastien Lecornu raised the health alert to its highest level to boost hospital staffing, while three nuclear plants in France have gone offline due to the heat.

Source: BBC

5. White House pressures OpenAI to limit GPT 5.6 release

OpenAI plans to share its newest model, GPT 5.6, only with a select group of close partners rather than the public, because the Trump administration told it to, according to The Information. At a meeting this week, CEO Sam Altman reportedly told staff that the government would be 'approving access customer by customer' during a preview period, with hopes for a broader release 'a couple of weeks later.' The agencies that reportedly asked for a limited release were the Office of the National Cyber Director and the Office of Science and Technology Policy. Earlier this month, Trump signed an executive order directing certain AI companies to voluntarily submit new models to the government for testing and evaluation before releasing them publicly.

Why it matters: The move signals the Trump administration - which originally positioned itself as taking a 'hands-off' approach to AI - is now pushing for federal oversight of new frontier models before public release.

Source: TechCrunch

Closer to Home

King Charles opens Scottish Parliament on Saturday

King Charles and Queen Camilla will be at Holyrood on Saturday to mark the official start of the 7th session of the Scottish Parliament, with pageantry, poetry and piping, plus speeches by the King, the presiding officer and the first minister. The ceremony follows a convention dating to 1 July 1999, when the first Royal Opening took place on the day the parliament officially received its powers, and each new session has started the same way since. Parliament chief executive David McGill said the Saturday date 'enables the public to be more actively involved in events to mark the occasion.' The Crown of Scotland - used at the coronation of Mary, Queen of Scots, in 1543 - will be part of the procession, and athletes due to compete at the Commonwealth Games in Glasgow later this summer will carry the Elizabeth Sword.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Bank Rate held at 3.75% in June 2026

The Monetary Policy Committee voted 7-2 to maintain Bank Rate at 3.75% at its meeting ending 17 June 2026. Two members voted to increase Bank Rate by 0.25 percentage points, to 4%. CPI inflation has fallen to 2.8% since the previous meeting, although it is expected to rise later this year as the effects of higher energy prices continue to pass through.

Source: Bank of England
