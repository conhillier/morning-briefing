1. Trump threatens Iran power plants and bridges

US President Donald Trump threatened to strike Iran's bridges and power plants next week if it did not return to talks, with the comments airing as the two countries exchanged fire for the fourth day in a row. "Next week it gets really bad for them," Trump said. "We're going to knock out all their power plants.

Why it matters: Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi said Trump's blockade decision "has, in a way, dismantled" an earlier agreed truce deal, and warned that if the US thinks tightening measures will bring Iran back to negotiations, "it is making a mistake."

Source: BBC

2. China GDP hits lowest since 2022

China's economy expanded by 4.3% in the second quarter, its weakest pace since the fourth quarter of 2022, missing economists' forecast for 4.5% growth and slowing from 5% in the first quarter. The result came below Beijing's full-year growth target range of 4.5% to 5%, the least ambitious goal in decades, amid sluggish domestic demand and volatile energy prices. Urban fixed-asset investment declined 5.7% in the first six months from a year earlier, worse than expectations for a 4.9% drop, with investment in real estate, infrastructure and manufacturing plunging 18%, 2.4% and 1.2% respectively. In June, retail sales grew 1%, rebounding from a 0.6% drop in the prior month, while industrial output expanded 5.3%.

Why it matters: Given the disappointing growth, Tianchen Xu, senior economist at Economist Intelligence Unit, expects stimulus measures to be ramped up in the third quarter, including a policy rate cut to stimulate investment demand.

Source: CNBC

3. Seven Britons confirmed dead in Spain wildfires

Seven Britons were among the 13 people killed in the deadly wildfires that tore through Spain's Almeria province on Thursday, Spanish authorities confirmed after completing post-mortem examinations. Twelve of the 13 victims were foreign nationals - seven from the United Kingdom, three from Belgium, one from France and one from the United States, with the last victim a Spanish citizen. "The 13 people who died are eight women and five men, all adults," the public body responsible for identifying the victims said. The fire, which has now been contained, burned through about 7,000 hectares (17,300 acres) of land, and nearly 1,500 people were evacuated from Almeria province.

Source: BBC

4. Goldman and JPMorgan post record AI-fuelled revenues

Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase each posted record quarterly revenue hauls fuelled by massive gains in equities trading and investment banking, with Goldman revenue jumping 39% to $20.3 billion and JPMorgan rising 27% to $58 billion. Revenue from equities trading rose 86% to $6 billion at JPMorgan and 72% to $7.42 billion at Goldman - a combined $4.4 billion more than analysts had expected.

Why it matters: Wells Fargo banking analyst Mike Mayo said the AI investment boom "reached a tipping point" in the second quarter, with Goldman, JPMorgan and Morgan Stanley identified as the top beneficiaries of this trend.

Source: CNBC

5. CMA orders heating oil compensation after Iran war price hikes

The Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) said 1,700 households were forced to "re-order at significantly higher prices or go without fuel" costing them up to £350 each, after heating oil suppliers cancelled orders when the Iran war broke out. Wholesale oil prices jumped from around $70 a barrel at the start of the Iran war in February to almost $120 a barrel by the end of March, with average retail prices rising at their peak by 92%. Some suppliers have agreed to compensate customers and the regulator is planning legal action against those who have so far refused. The CMA also found that some 1.5 million households depend on heating oil but do not have the same consumer protections as electricity and gas customers, with 60% of Northern Ireland households relying on it.

Source: BBC

Closer to Home

South East Water fined £30.5m for supply failures

South East Water will pay £30.5m after a series of supply interruptions, customer failings and breaching its licence, regulator Ofwat has said. The package includes a previously proposed £22m fine for water supply failures between 2020 and 2023 affecting more than 286,000 people, and follows a second investigation into further supply interruptions in Tunbridge Wells and across Kent and Sussex between November and January which left up to 70,000 homes without water. About half the fine, £13m, will be ringfenced for fixing issues that caused the supply failures, while £1.5m will go towards a community fund for areas in Kent and Sussex affected by failures. Ofwat will appoint an independent monitor to review South East Water's performance improvement plan.

Source: The Guardian

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, small companies and micro-entities must file profit and loss accounts with Companies House but may opt out of having this information published on the public register. All UK registered companies will be required to file accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software only, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings from that date. The implementation date has been moved from April 2027 to April 2028, giving companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
