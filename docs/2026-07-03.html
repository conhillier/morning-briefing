1. Kyiv hit by 'most massive' Russian attack

Ukraine's air force said Russia launched 74 missiles and 496 drones overnight, with 25 ballistic missiles and 12 drones striking 33 locations. Among the targets was a high-rise block of flats in Darnitskyi district, where two missiles caused devastation, and the Ukrainian Red Cross said its warehouse was destroyed with a loss of supplies worth more than £1.3m. The attack lasted more than 11 hours and came in several waves, starting with a drone strike on Kyiv's historic quarter before dozens of ballistic and cruise missiles were fired.

Why it matters: In the wake of the attacks, Ukraine urged allies to send more air defence systems, and Zelensky appealed to Washington to grant Ukraine licences to manufacture Patriot defence missiles.

Source: BBC

2. Venezuela quake toll rises to 2,595

Venezuela's earthquake death toll climbed to 2,595, Acting President Delcy Rodriguez announced, an increase of 300 from the previous day, as rescue operations continued more than a week after the country's strongest tremors in over a century. A magnitude 7.2 quake was followed seconds later by a 7.5 mainshock, the strongest tremors to hit Venezuela since 1900, flattening buildings across Caracas and La Guaira and severely damaging Simón Bolívar International Airport. The United Nations Development Programme projected direct physical damage at $6.7 billion, equivalent to about 6% of gross domestic product, while risk modeler Verisk estimated total economic losses of more than $10 billion. The US has mounted one of its largest disaster responses in years, deploying more than 900 personnel inside Venezuela, with the Trump administration pledging to mobilise $150 million in humanitarian aid.

Why it matters: According to an unofficial and widely circulated online list, some 38,500 people were still unaccounted for, down from nearly 60,000 in the days immediately after the quakes, suggesting the full scale of the disaster is still emerging.

Source: CNBC

3. US jobs growth misses forecasts in June

US employers added just 57,000 new jobs in June, roughly half of what economists had predicted, while the Bureau of Labor Statistics revised its figures from the past two months down by a total of 74,000. The unemployment rate dropped slightly to 4.2%, but 720,000 people left the labor force. The healthcare industry added 22,000 jobs in June, slower than its average monthly gain of 38,000, and the hospitality and leisure industry unexpectedly declined by 61,000, reflecting weaker than usual seasonal hiring despite World Cup soccer matches hosted across the US. The average number of jobs added over the last three months was about 111,000.

Why it matters: The latest jobs numbers make it all the more likely that the US Federal Reserve will continue to focus on inflation at its next meeting in late July; Fed officials have already projected that most members believe at least one rate hike will occur before the end of the year.

Source: The Guardian

4. Tesla posts record Q2 deliveries, beats forecasts

Tesla delivered 480,126 vehicles in the April to June period, a record for the second quarter and up about 25% from a year earlier, easily surpassing analysts' average estimate of 402,776 vehicles according to Visible Alpha data. The strong figures suggest Tesla's auto business is regaining momentum after two straight annual sales declines, with recovery in Europe aided by government EV incentives, faster electrification of corporate fleets, higher fuel prices and an easing of the consumer backlash over CEO Elon Musk's far-right politics. Tesla produced 451,758 vehicles during the quarter, with deliveries exceeding production by more than 28,000 vehicles, leading the company to draw down inventory built up in the first quarter. Tesla expects to spend more than $25bn on capital expenditure in 2026, nearly triple the $8.5bn spent last year, to expand AI infrastructure, battery production, Cybercab manufacturing and Optimus robots.

Source: The Guardian

5. OpenAI in talks to give US government 5% stake

OpenAI is reportedly in early stage talks to give a 5% stake in the ChatGPT developer to the US government, with CEO Sam Altman arguing this is the best way to share the benefits of AI, according to the Financial Times citing two unnamed people familiar with the discussions. The proposal would also involve other US AI companies giving a similar stake to the government, though it is not yet clear whether companies such as Anthropic, Google and Meta would agree. Altman and other OpenAI bosses have suggested that each of the biggest AI developers in the US should give 5% of their equity to an investment vehicle such as the Alaska Permanent Fund. The talks are described as "conceptual" and in early stages, and any deal could require an act of Congress to implement.

Why it matters: Such a deal would help improve the industry's relations with the Trump administration and could help garner political support by sharing wealth generated by the AI boom with the public.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Defra broke law over bee-killing pesticide

The UK government's Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs breached environmental law on four occasions when granting farmers emergency authorisation to use the banned neonicotinoid pesticide Cruiser SB on sugar beet crops in 2023 and 2024, the Office for Environmental Protection has concluded. The pesticide contains the active ingredient thiamethoxam, of which one teaspoon is enough to kill 1.25 billion honeybees, according to Prof Dave Goulson, a bee expert at the University of Sussex. The OEP found failures including a failure to consider the authorisation's impact on protected sites and to understand, avoid or mitigate the known risk of harm to those sites. In response, Defra has proposed to update the assessment process for granting emergency authorisations, with the updated process due to be in place by November 2026.

Source: The Guardian
