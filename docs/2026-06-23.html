1. US eases Iran oil sanctions, inspectors row erupts

The US temporarily waived sanctions, allowing Iran to sell oil in US dollars for the first time in decades, following the first round of talks in Switzerland. The 60-day emergency licence authorises the production, sale and delivery of Iranian crude and petrochemicals until 21 August, and even allows Iranian oil to be imported directly into the US. However, Iran's foreign ministry told state media that Tehran had made "no new commitments" on nuclear inspections, directly contradicting Vice-President JD Vance's claim that inspectors could return "as soon as today". Iran's Foreign Minister Seyed Abbas Araghchi said the first "real test" would be Lebanon.

Why it matters: Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the waiver was issued in exchange for Tehran committing to keep the Strait of Hormuz open and allowing IAEA nuclear inspectors back into the country - but Iran's denial of that commitment puts the fragile deal under immediate strain.

Source: BBC

2. Qatar LNG blast kills 13, exports unaffected

At least 13 people have been killed and 66 injured after an explosion at Qatar's largest gas facility in the Ras Laffan industrial zone on Sunday night. Qatar's Energy Minister Saad Sherida al-Kaabi confirmed those killed were all from India and Pakistan, and said the explosion would not affect the country's exports, describing it as "an accident and not sabotage or hostile in nature". The blast rattled windows and was felt across central Doha, more than 70km from Ras Laffan. The minister said plant production had been intentionally stopped since December 2025 for urgent maintenance and had only been restarted two days before the explosion.

Why it matters: The energy minister said it would be difficult to determine when operations would resume, and the Ras Laffan facility - the world's largest LNG export facility - had already seen output reduced by 12.8m tons of LNG for three to five years following earlier damage from retaliatory strikes during the US-Israel war with Iran.

Source: BBC

3. Oracle cuts 21,000 jobs, blames AI shift

Oracle shed about 21,000 roles globally in the last year, with the firm's latest annual report showing it had around 141,000 full-time employees as of 31 May 2026, down from about 162,000 at the same time last year. Oracle warned the reorganisation may lead to a shortage of skilled workers in certain roles, resulting in a loss of productivity.

Why it matters: More than 100,000 tech workers have been laid off in the past year across the industry, according to estimates from employment tracking firms, as companies including Amazon and Meta cut thousands of jobs while investing heavily in AI infrastructure.

Source: BBC

4. Europe heatwave: UK issues rare red warning

Britain's Met Office issued a rare red weather warning covering an area stretching from London to Swansea and Somerset to Birmingham, in effect from 9am on Wednesday to 9pm on Thursday, as temperatures are forecast to reach 38-40C in parts of England and Wales. The Met Office said this heatwave was expected to bring "severe and significant impacts" including widespread health risks for many - not just those normally more vulnerable to heat - and even danger to life. The alert also warned that "substantial changes" in working practices and daily routines would be required, and indicated a high risk of failure of heat-sensitive systems, including the loss of power, water, electricity, gas or mobile phone services. The forecast temperatures would smash the June record set in 1976 by several degrees.

Why it matters: In France, a heatwave red alert has been issued to more than half of the country's departments, affecting about 39 million people, and at least 18 have died - including two children left in a hot car - since the weekend, with French prime minister Sebastien Lecornu scheduled to hold a crisis meeting on Tuesday.

Source: The Guardian

5. NHS approves first drug to delay type 1 diabetes

The world's first drug to delay the onset of type 1 diabetes, teplizumab, is to be made available on the NHS in England and Wales after the National Institute for Health and Care Excellence (Nice) gave the drug the green light on Tuesday. The immunotherapy does not cure type 1 diabetes but postpones its onset for as long as three years, and has been approved for use in adults and children aged eight and above with early, pre-symptomatic type 1 diabetes (stage 2). Teplizumab, also known as tzield and made by Sanofi, trains the immune system to stop attacking pancreatic cells and is given through a drip once a day for 14 days in a row. The only other treatment for the disease - insulin - was discovered 105 years ago and does not alter the course of the disease.

Why it matters: Diabetes UK said the approval marks the start of "a new age of type 1 diabetes treatment", stating that "for the first time in 100 years, we are moving beyond insulin, with a medicine that targets the root cause of the condition."

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Murrell sentenced today for SNP embezzlement

Former SNP chief executive Peter Murrell is to be sentenced at the High Court in Edinburgh on Tuesday after admitting embezzling more than £400,000 from the party. At a court hearing on 25 May, Murrell admitted embezzling £400,310.65 from the SNP between 2010 and 2022, using charge cards, bank transfers and fake invoices to illicitly purchase items including jewellery, cosmetics, stationery, games consoles, cars, a motorhome, kitchenware and toiletries. The 61-year-old - the estranged husband of former First Minister Nicola Sturgeon - has been remanded in custody since pleading guilty last month and is facing a substantial jail term. Sturgeon has denied any knowledge of his wrongdoing, saying she was "deceived, betrayed and lied to".

Source: BBC
