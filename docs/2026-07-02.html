1. Russia's 'most massive' Kyiv attack kills 13

Russian forces launched a major drone and missile attack on Kyiv overnight, killing 13 people in what the city's mayor described as the "most massive attack" on the Ukrainian capital. Mayor Vitaly Klitschko declared Friday a day of mourning and said around 90 people were injured, with an ambulance station among the places hit. Ukraine's air force said Russia had launched 74 missiles and 496 drones overnight, mainly targeting the capital; while most were repelled, 25 ballistic missiles and 12 drones struck 33 locations. Children were among the "significant number" of casualties, according to the head of Kyiv's military administration.

Why it matters: The attack marks the first large-scale missile and drone strike by Russia on Ukraine in more than two weeks, prompting Ukraine's foreign minister to call on partners for more air defence systems and increased sanctions on Russia.

Source: BBC

2. US-Iran Doha talks end with no breakthrough

US and Iranian negotiators met separately on Wednesday with Qatari and Pakistani mediators in Doha, with Qatar describing "positive progress" but no sign the sides had bridged key differences. Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner represented the US, while Iran's Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi led Tehran's technical team; Iran's top negotiators did not attend. Discussions covered the release of $6 billion in frozen Iranian funds, the Strait of Hormuz, Lebanon, and Iran's nuclear programme. The next meeting will be scheduled after six-day funeral processions for former Iranian Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, set to begin on Saturday.

Why it matters: Control of the Strait of Hormuz remains a key sticking point, with Iran insisting on controlling vessel routes and charging fees for passage - a position the US and many Gulf Arab states refuse to accept.

Source: NPR

3. Amnesty: Sudan's RSF committed crimes against humanity in El Fasher

Amnesty International has alleged that Sudan's Rapid Support Forces committed crimes against humanity and ethnic cleansing during their campaign to capture El Fasher, the last stronghold of the Sudan armed forces in the Darfur region. The report, based on interviews with 247 people including 208 survivors, found the RSF committed murder, torture, rape, enslavement and sexual slavery as part of a widespread and systematic attack against civilians between mid-2024 and late 2025. Amnesty also found that the RSF deliberately targeted children and repeatedly used derogatory and dehumanising language, committing the crime against humanity of persecution on the basis of ethnicity. The report named three RSF commanders it said were responsible for serious violations of international law.

Why it matters: Amnesty's secretary general Agnes Callamard called on the international community to "move beyond statements of concern and take concrete steps to protect civilians, breaking the cycle of impunity", and called for an immediate ceasefire and urgent deployment of an international protection force.

Source: The Guardian

4. Google must pay EU's record 4.1bn euro Android fine

Europe's top court has dismissed Google's appeal and ruled the company must pay a €4.1 billion (£3.5 billion) fine for using its Android mobile operating system to block rivals - the largest penalty the European Commission has ever imposed against Google. The original €4.3 billion fine was handed down in 2018 and trimmed to €4.1 billion in 2022. The fine was based on three alleged illegal practices: requiring Android device manufacturers to pre-install Google Search and Chrome as a condition of Play Store access, paying manufacturers to exclusively pre-install Google Search, and preventing manufacturers from selling devices running alternative "forked" versions of Android. A Google spokesperson said the judgement "fails to recognise" the firm's "significant investment to ensure Android remains open, interoperable and free".

Source: BBC

5. UN warns AI risks deepening global inequality

A new United Nations report warns that the development of artificial intelligence may exacerbate global inequality and proposes a shared framework for responsible AI development, as adoption grows unevenly across the world. The report states that while more than a billion people now use AI weekly, "adoption across the global south lagging far behind the global north", with the US and China dominating in both leading AI model development and investment in compute infrastructure.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Police Scotland officer jailed 10 years for rape

A Police Scotland officer, Cameron Ross, has been jailed for 10 years after being found guilty of raping two women and subjecting a third to a campaign of abuse. Ross carried out attacks in Stornoway on the Isle of Lewis in 2012 and 2014, before abusing another woman in Inverness between 2019 and 2022. The 39-year-old had denied the charges but was found guilty by a jury during a trial at the High Court in Edinburgh in May. He was suspended from Police Scotland in January 2024 after the allegations came to light and resigned last month.

Source: BBC
