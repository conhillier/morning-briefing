1. Russia kills 11 in massive Kyiv strike

Russian forces launched a large-scale attack on Ukraine's capital overnight into Thursday, killing 11 people and injuring 54, including two children, according to Interior Minister Ihor Klymenko and city military chief Tymur Tkachenko. The attack with ballistic and cruise missiles and drones damaged 20 residential buildings, with damage recorded in 30 locations across the city. In the Darnytskyi district, six levels of a nine-story building collapsed after a Russian strike, and a paramedic in extremely critical condition was among the injured in the Shevchenkivskyi district. Foreign Minister Andrii Sybiha called on Ukraine's allies to strengthen the country's air defenses following what he described as a "night of horror" in Kyiv.

Why it matters: Sybiha warned that the death toll may rise as rescue teams continued their work, and urged partners not to delay decisions on supplying air defense systems and missiles.

Source: NPR

2. German prosecutors charge Ukrainian over Nord Stream blasts

German federal prosecutors have filed charges against a 50-year-old Ukrainian national, identified only as Serhii K, over the 2022 explosions that destroyed the Nord Stream 1 and Nord Stream 2 underwater gas pipelines. Prosecutors allege that Serhii K helped coordinate a team that used a sailing yacht, the Andromeda, to place explosive devices on the pipelines near Denmark's Bornholm Island in September 2022, acting as the on-board coordinator and team leader rather than as a diver or bomb expert. Federal prosecutors confirmed Serhii K was the same suspect arrested in August 2025 in Italy and extradited to Germany in November, previously named as Serhii Kuznietsov. Kuznietsov has denied being part of the sabotage operation, saying he was a member of the Ukrainian armed forces and in Ukraine at the time.

Why it matters: The underwater explosions damaged both pipelines so severely that no gas could be transported through them, knocking out key routes for Russian gas to Europe for months after Moscow's full-scale invasion of Ukraine in February 2022.

Source: Al Jazeera

3. Amnesty: Sudan's RSF committed crimes against humanity in El Fasher

Amnesty International has alleged that Sudan's Rapid Support Forces committed crimes against humanity and ethnic cleansing during their campaign to capture El Fasher, the last stronghold of Sudan's armed forces in the Darfur region. For the report, Amnesty interviewed 247 people, including 208 survivors of fighting in the city and surrounding areas, and also analysed documentary and video material and satellite imagery from North Darfur. The report concluded the RSF committed war crimes between mid-2024 and late 2025, repeatedly targeting non-Arab civilians and using derogatory and dehumanising language, with the destruction of towns including Abu Zerega between December 2024 and March 2025 consistent with ethnic cleansing. The report named three RSF commanders responsible for serious violations: Maj Gen Gedo Hamdan Ahmed Mohamed, Lt Col Abbas Khater Bakhit, and commander Al-Fateh Abdullah Idris.

Why it matters: Amnesty called for an immediate ceasefire and the urgent deployment of an international force to protect civilians, with secretary general Agnes Callamard stating: "The international community must move beyond statements of concern and take concrete steps to protect civilians, breaking the cycle of impunity."

Source: The Guardian

4. Anthropic export ban lifted on Fable and Mythos AI models

Anthropic has restored customer access to its AI model Fable 5 after a more than two-week blackout following US government safety concerns that it could be abused to enable serious cyber-attacks. Anthropic said researchers from the US Department of Commerce's Center for AI Standards and Innovation tested both prior and new safeguards and agreed they are "extraordinarily strong".

Source: The Guardian

5. UN warns AI could deepen global inequality

A new United Nations report warns that the development of artificial intelligence may exacerbate global inequality and proposes a shared framework for responsible AI development, as adoption and investment accelerates unevenly across the world. The report from the independent international scientific panel on AI states that while more than a billion people now use AI weekly, "adoption across the global south lagging far behind the global north", with the US and China dominating in the development of leading AI models and investment into compute infrastructure. The report warns that "the concentration of AI capabilities in a small number of firms and countries could enable authoritarian capture and undermine democratic accountability", and notes that more than 2 billion people - almost a third of the global population - are completely offline.

Why it matters: UN Secretary General Antonio Guterres said at a press conference: "The more AI advances without shared rules, the less say governments and people will have in the outcome," urging governments not to wait.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Scots women sue Pfizer over Depo-Provera brain tumours

About 150 women in Scotland are at the early stages of taking legal action against drug manufacturer Pfizer after being diagnosed with brain tumours they believe were caused by the contraceptive injection Depo-Provera. Nurse Nicola McKay, 53, from Portmahomak in Easter Ross, says she has met dozens of other women with the same tumours who took the Depo-Provera injection over a long period of time; she was diagnosed with three non-cancerous brain tumours known as meningiomas in February last year after having five seizures. A 2024 study published by the British Medical Journal found women who used Depo-Provera for at least 12 months were 5.6 times more likely to develop meningioma brain tumours, and the same year a patient information leaflet was updated by the UK medicines regulator and Pfizer to warn about brain tumours. The women met with Scottish Labour's Jackie Baillie at Holyrood to discuss calls for a UK-wide inquiry into the link between Depo-Provera and meningioma brain tumours.

Source: BBC
