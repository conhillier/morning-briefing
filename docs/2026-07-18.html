1. US strikes Iran for seventh night running

The US military carried out its seventh consecutive night of strikes on Iran since President Donald Trump declared the ceasefire agreement was "over". US Central Command said its forces "hit surveillance sites, military logistics infrastructure, underground weapons storage, and maritime capabilities", employing fighter aircraft, aerial drones, and warships. Iran claimed to have responded by targeting US allies including Kuwait, whose army said its air defences had been "confronting hostile missile and drone attacks" following "Iranian aggression". Jordan's armed forces also said it intercepted 10 Iranian missiles fired into its airspace overnight, though no damage was reported.

Why it matters: Shipping traffic in the vital Strait of Hormuz has largely stopped amid the continuing strikes; the strait normally accounts for about one-fifth of the world's oil and liquefied natural gas supplies.

Source: BBC

2. Burnham takes Labour leadership, pledges unity

Andy Burnham pledged to lead a united Labour government free of infighting as he was confirmed as party leader at a special conference at the TUC headquarters in London on Friday. He set out a distinctly leftwing vision, promising to undo the Thatcherism of the 1980s, bring in more public ownership of utilities, and build a new generation of council homes, while also insisting he would be "pro-business". Burnham, who will become prime minister on Monday, said this was Labour's "last chance to change" and the country was "crying out for a new politics". Although no final decisions have been made, Shabana Mahmood is the frontrunner to enter the Treasury ahead of the left's choice, Ed Miliband.

Why it matters: Burnham is already facing tensions within the party over his cabinet choices, with one senior MP describing the prospect of Mahmood as chancellor as "mad" and "not the answer".

Source: The Guardian

3. South East Water warns of survival threat

South East Water has warned there is "material uncertainty" over its survival, saying it has sufficient funds only to make it through to July 2027, after which it will need new loan facilities to continue as a going concern. The water supplier to 2.4 million customers across Kent, Sussex, Surrey, Hampshire and Berkshire revealed losses had widened to £33m, up from £14m last year, despite revenues soaring from £285m to £352m after Ofwat allowed it to increase bills by 7%. The company is already struggling with £80m a year in finance costs and raised the prospect of borrowing from hedge funds and private debt investors if it cannot secure mainstream lending. Chief executive David Hinton received £488,000 in total pay in the period, even after forgoing his bonus under pressure from MPs.

Why it matters: South East Water's struggles underline the challenge the water industry poses for incoming prime minister Andy Burnham, who is considering putting Thames Water into special administration.

Source: The Guardian

4. China's Moonshot AI unveils giant Kimi K3 model

Chinese AI start-up Moonshot has unveiled Kimi K3, a model containing 2.8 trillion parameters, which it says can rival top American firms OpenAI and Anthropic. The model will be released as open-source on 27 July, making it the world's first open-source model in the three-trillion-parameter class that can be freely downloaded, run and customised by outside developers. Third-party evaluations from Artificial Analysis and Arena.ai show the model performing on a par with leading US models, with Kimi K3 ranking first in web interface engineering and outperforming Anthropic's Fable system in blind human-preference tests. The announcement caused shares in Moonshot's domestic competitors Zhipu and MiniMax to tumble by about 27% and 16% respectively in Hong Kong.

Why it matters: The rapid arrival of Kimi K3 suggests Chinese firms are successfully bypassing US regulatory barriers and advancing independently despite US restrictions on hardware sales.

Source: BBC

5. White House seizes control of frontier AI access

The Trump administration has taken new steps to assert control over the rollout of future AI model releases by dictating which companies and entities are allowed access to the latest frontier models, according to two people familiar with the matter who spoke to CNBC. The administration this week launched its own programme, dubbed "Gold Eagle", aimed at collaborating with the private sector to find and fix cyber vulnerabilities, with the so-called clearinghouse putting the White House in charge of greenlighting which companies can access new AI models. Last month the Trump administration blocked Claude Mythos 5 and Fable 5 due to "national security concerns", reinstating access after weeks of intense negotiations with Anthropic. OpenAI last month said it would limit new AI models to "trusted partners" to comply with government requests.

Why it matters: Going forward, according to one person, these rollouts will require explicit government approval for which partners can be involved, leaving the future of company-led initiatives such as Project Glasswing and OpenAI's Daybreak in doubt.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

Cairngorms wildfire still raging after three days

A wildfire that has been ablaze for three days in the Cairngorms is still "burning hard", with community leaders criticising the Scottish government for describing it as "contained" while flames continue to spread through the national park. The Scottish Fire and Rescue Service said four square kilometres of land had been torched since Wednesday, with more than 50 firefighters, nine fire engines, two specialist wildfire units and two helicopters deployed on Friday. Homes, campsites and a ski resort in the Glenmore Forest Park area have been evacuated as a safety precaution, and RSPB Scotland said about 300 hectares of its reserve at Loch Garten, Abernethy, had been burned.

Source: BBC
