1. US and Iran sign peace deal at Versailles

The presidents of the US and Iran have signed an initial peace deal aiming to end the war, allowing it to immediately take effect. The agreement includes reopening the Strait of Hormuz, a $300bn plan for Iran's "reconstruction", and the US terminating "all types of sanctions" on Iran. Trump signed the hard copy of the memorandum of understanding during a state dinner hosted by French President Emmanuel Macron at the Palace of Versailles. The nuclear issue remains unresolved, with the text committing both sides to "negotiating and achieving the final deal in maximum 60 days, extendable with mutual consent".

Why it matters: Iran's parliamentary speaker Ghalibaf warned that the Strait of Hormuz "will not return to pre-war conditions" and suggested Iran would charge ships crossing the passage after the 60-day period lapses, leaving the deal's durability in question.

Source: BBC

2. Iranian tankers break US blockade after deal

Three Iranian tankers loaded with crude oil have passed the US blockade line in the Gulf of Oman, ship-tracking data shows. The three Iran-flagged tankers -- Diona, Hero II, and Sonia I -- are all owned by the National Iranian Tanker Company and have been sanctioned by the US Treasury. The ships are carrying a combined total of 3.8 million barrels of crude oil, according to TankerTrackers.com, and are currently not broadcasting their planned destinations. Maritime intelligence firm Windward says this is the first time any of these Iranian tankers have broadcast their locations since March.

Why it matters: If the tankers make it to their destination, they will be Iran's first oil exports for two months, according to TankerTrackers.com, signalling a rapid resumption of Iranian oil trade even before the deal is formally signed.

Source: BBC

3. Fed holds rates as inflation hits 3.8%

The Federal Reserve held US interest rates between 3.5% and 3.75% after Kevin Warsh's first meeting in charge of the central bank. With inflation running at an above-target 3.8%, and uncertainty surrounding Trump's deal to end the war with Iran, the Fed's rate-setting committee unanimously decided to keep rates steady. Nine of the 18 central bankers who participated in the rate-setting process predicted an interest rate hike this year, while just one said they expected a cut. Warsh also launched task forces to examine five areas of how the Fed works, including how it communicates and the size of its balance sheet.

Why it matters: The Fed's update removed a statement hinting that it was leaning towards lowering interest rates in the future, and its slimmed-down statement of just 132 words -- down from almost 350 in April -- signals a major shift in how the central bank communicates.

Source: BBC

4. Oil falls as IEA warns of supply glut

Oil prices declined Thursday after Trump signed a deal with Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian, while the International Energy Agency flagged a supply glut next year. International benchmark Brent crude futures for August dropped 1.13% to $78.65 a barrel, and US West Texas Intermediate futures for July fell 1.26% to $75.82 per barrel. The IEA expects a lasting resolution to the conflict will result in significantly higher supply volumes and spark a major oil overhang next year, with global supply expected to recover to 110.3 mb/d in 2027. Trump also told reporters he could resume attacks on Iran if Tehran failed to honour its commitments.

Source: CNBC

5. G7 leaders fear US could cut off AI access

At the G7 Summit, French President Emmanuel Macron warned that if the US "from one day to the next can turn off the switch," it could harm European economies and damage AI firms themselves. The comments followed the Trump administration blocking Anthropic from exporting its newest Mythos 5 and Fable 5 models on national security grounds after Amazon flagged that certain safety guardrails could be bypassed. Indian Prime Minister Narendra Modi also expressed concern, saying democratic nations must have unfettered access to top AI models to protect critical infrastructure. G7 leaders discussed the creation of a "trusted partners" scheme that would grant non-US nations access to advanced AI models from firms like Anthropic and OpenAI.

Why it matters: Canadian enterprise AI firm Cohere's CEO Aidan Gomez warned that dependence on a small handful of big tech companies is "dangerous to resilience", arguing the episode is fundamentally about "who controls the foundational technology that will shape our economic security and national sovereignty for decades to come".

Source: TechCrunch

Closer to Home

Scottish by-elections under way today

Voters are going to the polls for two by-elections on Scotland's east coast, in Aberdeen South and the Arbroath and Broughty Ferry constituency. The contests were triggered following the election of SNP MPs Stephen Flynn and Stephen Gethins to Holyrood last month, who had to resign their Westminster seats to take up their Holyrood places. The SNP's rivals are hoping to overturn Flynn's majority of almost 4,000 votes in Aberdeen South, and Gethins' majority of about 900 votes in Arbroath and Broughty Ferry. Polls are open from 07:00 to 22:00 with results expected to be announced in the early hours of Friday.

Source: BBC
