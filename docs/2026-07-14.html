1. UAE condemns Iran tanker strikes, US imposes Hormuz blockade

The UAE accused Iran of a "brazen" attack on two national tankers in the Strait of Hormuz on Monday, killing an Indian crew member and wounding eight others, four of whom suffered serious injuries. Six of those injured were Indian and two were Ukrainian, according to the UAE Ministry of Defence. Donald Trump announced the US would reimpose a naval blockade of Iranian ports and impose a 20% charge on all cargo shipped through the Strait, effective from 16:00 Eastern Time on Tuesday 14 July. US Central Command confirmed it struck military targets across Iran, including Bushehr, Chah Bahar, Jask, Konarak, Abu Musa, and Bandar Abbas, to "further degrade Iran's ability to attack commercial shipping".

Why it matters: Iran's foreign minister responded by declaring Tehran would remain the Strait's "GUARDIAN" forever, and the IRGC warned that co-operation with the "aggressor enemy" would lead to "creation of an energy crisis in the world".

Source: BBC

2. Oil hits one-month high as Hormuz traffic collapses

Brent crude rose 2 percent on Tuesday, extending a 9.6 percent gain from the previous day, with Brent futures for September delivery standing at $84.91 a barrel as of 03:30 GMT - the highest since 15 June. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has plummeted, with only 57 transits recorded from Friday through Sunday, a more than 50 percent drop compared with the previous week, down from roughly 130 vessels daily before the US and Israel launched their initial strikes on Iran in late February. One analyst at TD Securities said a move to $100 a barrel was "quite possible, should it become apparent that physical shortage risks are real and increasingly likely". An analyst at Sparta Commodities warned that "crude oil is fast losing its strategic petroleum reserve buffer, and a violent repricing up cannot be discounted until the market sees toned-down rhetoric from both parties".

Why it matters: One oil market analyst told Al Jazeera that "much of that cushion has now been depleted, leaving us much more vulnerable to a rerun of March and April", suggesting the market has less capacity to absorb further supply shocks.

Source: Al Jazeera

3. Bangkok bar fire death toll rises to 30

The death toll from the fire at Rong Beer Na Lat Phrao in Bangkok's Chatuchak district has risen to at least 30, with more than 70 people injured, 24 of them critically. Preliminary investigations suggest the blaze was caused by a short circuit in an air conditioner, which quickly cut power throughout the bar. A fire safety expert found that the door near the restrooms was locked, while two doors at the entrance were partially obstructed by furniture, trapping customers who instinctively fled towards the back. The bar was registered as a "restaurant with live music" rather than an "entertainment venue", meaning it was not required to use fire-retardant materials.

Why it matters: Bangkok's Metropolitan Administration said it is reviewing regulations governing the materials allowed in the construction and decoration of entertainment venues and restaurants, following findings that the stage was decorated with highly flammable plastic flowers and combustible foam on the ceiling.

Source: BBC

4. China June exports surge at fastest pace since 2021

China's overall exports rose 27% from a year earlier in US dollar value terms in June, the strongest pace since October 2021, accelerating from the 19.4% gain in May and sharply beating economists' estimates for 18.2% growth. Imports grew 36% in June, the largest jump since June 2021, with the trade surplus standing at $125.6 billion. Chinese exports of integrated circuits more than doubled from the same period last year to $38 billion, reflecting the global AI investment boom. However, China's crude imports dropped 41% from a year earlier to 29.3 million tons, reportedly the lowest level in nearly a decade.

Why it matters: Manufacturers are bracing for additional tariffs from US President Donald Trump's Section 301 probes as a 10% broad-based duty is set to expire on 24 July, and exports will likely remain strong in the second half of the year, potentially further increasing trade tensions particularly with Europe.

Source: CNBC

Closer to Home

Sturgeon's police statement will not be released

Nicola Sturgeon's lawyer Aamer Anwar confirmed on Monday that neither her written statement to Police Scotland nor her police interview from the Operation Branchform investigation into SNP finances will be released. After her arrest in 2023, Sturgeon exercised her right to give a "no comment" police interview and later submitted a written statement to officers. The inquiry ultimately led to her former husband Peter Murrell being convicted of embezzling more than PS400,000 from the SNP and being jailed for five years and three months in June. Sturgeon was told last year that she was no longer under investigation, and her lawyer said "there is no onus on her to now prove her innocence in the court of media opinion".
