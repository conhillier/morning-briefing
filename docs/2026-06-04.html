1. Iranian Drones Kill One at Kuwait Airport

Iranian drones heavily damaged a passenger terminal at Kuwait International Airport on Wednesday, killing one person and wounding 63 others, including passengers and workers. Kuwait's Defense Ministry said it destroyed over a dozen missiles and a similar number of drones from Iran. Iran denied responsibility, with its Revolutionary Guard claiming the damage was caused by a failed US interceptor, a claim US Central Command called false. Kuwait expelled two Iranian diplomats in response, and the airport partially reopened at a different terminal with Kuwait Airways flights resuming.

Why it matters: The strike reinforced the risks to residents and travelers in Gulf countries that had considered themselves relative havens before the US-Israeli war with Iran.

Source: NPR

2. US House Votes to Curb Trump Iran War Powers

The US House of Representatives passed a measure 215-208 seeking to halt President Donald Trump from taking further military action in Iran, with four Republicans joining Democrats. This was the fourth attempt by the House to rein in Trump's war powers, which critics say lack congressional approval. The resolution still needs approval from the Republican-controlled Senate, and even if passed there, it is unlikely to fully curb military action. Trump told reporters on Wednesday that negotiations to end the war are going 'very well' and could be finalised 'as soon as this weekend.'

Why it matters: The vote marked the latest sign of division within Trump's Republican Party on the Iran conflict.

Source: BBC

3. SpaceX Sets $135 IPO Price at $1.77 Trillion Valuation

Elon Musk's SpaceX set a fixed IPO price of $135 per share, implying a valuation of $1.77 trillion, according to a filing with the SEC on Wednesday. The company plans to sell 555.6 million shares, amounting to a $75 billion fundraise, with underwriters holding an option for an additional 83.33 million shares worth $11.2 billion. SpaceX is planning to debut on the Nasdaq on June 12 under the ticker SPCX, which would make it the biggest IPO ever, more than triple the size of Alibaba. Musk will own over 82% voting control after the offering.

Why it matters: At $1.77 trillion, SpaceX would become the seventh-biggest company in the US by market cap, putting it above Tesla, which is valued at about $1.6 trillion.

Source: CNBC

4. Ukraine Drones Strike St Petersburg Before Putin Forum

Ukraine carried out a drone strike on the outskirts of St Petersburg hours before the opening of a major economic forum, with local authorities saying air defences shot down 59 drones overnight and that three different districts of the city were hit. Volodymyr Zelensky confirmed Ukrainian drones hit several locations including an oil terminal and a naval base in Kronstadt, the main outpost of the Russian Navy's Baltic Fleet. The commander of Ukraine's unmanned systems said the corvette Boikiy was struck. Kremlin spokesman Dmitry Peskov said Moscow would respond, stating: 'Our responses will be systemic in nature.'

Why it matters: The St Petersburg Economic Forum, once dubbed the 'Russian Davos,' is designed to attract foreign investment, and the strikes directly targeted the event's backdrop, including energy infrastructure Russia relies on to fund its war effort.

Source: BBC

5. ADF Rebel Attacks Kill 30, Worsen DRC Ebola Crisis

Rebel attacks around the city of Beni in North Kivu, eastern DRC, have left more than 30 people dead over the past few days, with at least 10 massacred in raids on three villages in the early hours of Wednesday morning. The Allied Democratic Forces (ADF), a militia affiliated to Islamic State, has been blamed for the attacks. As of Wednesday, 344 cases and 60 deaths have been recorded in the Ebola outbreak across North Kivu, South Kivu and Ituri provinces. The military governor of North Kivu confirmed that three patients with Ebola had fled treatment centres in Beni following the Saturday attacks.

Why it matters: Community leaders warned that people fleeing violence increases the risk of Ebola spreading, as containment measures cannot be enforced during flight.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Glasgow Maternity Ward Ordered to Make 26 Improvements

Staff at the Queen Elizabeth University Hospital (QEUH) maternity unit in Glasgow described conditions as 'unsafe' and 'dangerous' during an inspection by Healthcare Improvement Scotland (HIS), which ordered 26 improvements. Inspectors found inpatient wards regularly functioning at between 7% and 13% over capacity, delays of up to 190 hours for induction of labour at the longest, and emergency trolleys containing expired equipment and medication. HIS also raised serious concerns about cleanliness, including mould around windows and sharps disposal bins contaminated with blood. NHS Greater Glasgow and Clyde's director of midwifery apologised and said 55 more midwives will have joined the team by October.

Source: BBC
