1. Scotland beat Haiti in World Cup return

John McGinn's deflected goal gave Scotland a 1-0 win over Haiti at the Gillette Stadium near Boston, marking their first World Cup victory since a 2-1 defeat of Sweden in 1990 and their first appearance at the tournament since 1998. The stadium was filled to its 64,000 capacity with a majority of Scottish fans as McGinn fired in with the aid of a deflection just before the half-hour mark. The result puts Scotland top of Group C, with Steve Clarke's side now aiming to make history by reaching the knockout stages, something Scotland have never done at the World Cup. They next face Morocco at the same stadium on Friday before heading to Miami to take on Brazil.

Why it matters: It is also Scotland's first victory at any major tournament in 30 years, since Euro 96, and the win puts them on course to reach the last 32, especially as the eight best third-placed sides will go through from the group stage.

Source: Al Jazeera

2. SpaceX IPO: Musk becomes first trillionaire

SpaceX closed Friday with a market cap above $2 trillion, after raising $75 billion in the largest IPO on record - roughly triple the size of the next-biggest US offering, which was Alibaba's in 2014. The stock was priced at $135 with no price range used to gauge demand, and closed up 19% at the end of its first day of trading. More than 500 million shares changed hands, and the debut pushed Alphabet's stake in SpaceX past the $100 billion mark after the company invested about $900 million in SpaceX in 2015. The IPO also reportedly minted some 4,400 millionaires among the ranks of current and former SpaceX employees.

Why it matters: SpaceX's successful debut lifted confidence in potential deals later this year from AI model giants OpenAI and Anthropic, which are each valued at close to $1 trillion on the private market, with former Nasdaq CEO Robert Greifeld saying he "would definitely bet" that both will go public in 2026.

Source: CNBC

3. Anthropic disables top AI models on US order

Anthropic announced it disabled access to its Fable 5 and Mythos 5 AI models after receiving a government export control directive citing "national security authorities" at 5:21 p.m. ET on Friday. The order instructed Anthropic to suspend all access to the models "by any foreign national, whether inside or outside the United States, including foreign national Anthropic employees." Anthropic abruptly disabled the models for all of its customers to ensure compliance, though said all of its other models would not be affected. In its statement, Anthropic said the government did not provide specific details about its national security concern and apologised to customers for the disruption.

Why it matters: Anthropic stated that "this action does not adhere" to the principles of a statutory process it believes should be "transparent, fair, clear, and grounded in technical facts", marking the company's latest run-in with the US government after the Department of Defense previously declared Anthropic a supply chain risk.

Source: CNBC

4. G7 summit dominated by Iran war tensions

President Trump travels to Evian, France, next week for the G7 Summit, which has been overtaken by concerns over surging energy costs and differences over the escalating conflict with Iran. Tensions between the US and its G7 allies exploded into public view when Trump announced the withdrawal of at least 5,000 US troops from Germany, a decision that was partially reversed with the troops redirected to Poland. The United Kingdom is working with France to build a coalition of nations to help with the demining of the Strait of Hormuz once a peace deal is reached. Analyst Brett Bruen, who will be meeting with G7 officials at the summit, warned: "There is no doubt that Iran is going to dominate the agenda at Evian."

Why it matters: Analysts say the friction has had unintended consequences, with repeated US pressure over trade, defense spending and security policy pushing European governments closer together and leading to greater strategic autonomy.

Source: NPR

5. Tren de Aragua leader killed in US strike

The US military killed Hector Rusthenford Guerrero Flores, leader of the Venezuelan street gang Tren de Aragua, in a strike coordinated with Venezuela, President Trump announced on Friday.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Anti-racism rally disrupted in Glasgow

Thousands of anti-racism campaigners marched along Buchanan Street in Glasgow city centre on Saturday but were met by several dozen far-right demonstrators who clashed with police. The black-clad masked group - including at least one performing a Nazi-style salute - scuffled with officers as campaigners shouted "off our streets", with projectiles including eggs and drink cans thrown. Police Scotland said the rally passed "without major incident" with only one arrest - a 53-year-old man who had threatened an officer - and that several individuals were issued with city centre dispersal orders. The rally came after race-related attacks in Glasgow earlier in the week, following a knife attack in Belfast on Monday which left a man with serious injuries.

Source: BBC
