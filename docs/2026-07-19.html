1. US hits Iran for eighth night running

The United States military launched its eighth consecutive night of strikes on Iran, targeting coastal surveillance and air defence facilities, maritime capabilities, and missile and drone storage sites. Strikes hit an area near Sirik in Iran's Hormozgan province at 1:30am local time and Qeshm Island at 3:38am and again at 6:10am local time. The attacks came in retaliation for Iranian ballistic missile and drone strikes on a US base in Jordan that killed two US service members, with one still missing and four requiring hospitalisation. Iranian authorities said at least 50 people have been killed and more than 500 wounded in US strikes in the past three weeks.

Why it matters: Iran's supreme leader warned of "unforgettable lessons" if the US continued its attacks, and US strikes have widened to include civilian infrastructure including bridges and desalination plants, cutting off water supplies to about 10,000 people.

Source: Al Jazeera

2. Iran strikes back at US bases in Kuwait

Iran's army carried out drone attacks on two US bases in Kuwait in response to the latest American strikes, according to state media. Washington and Tehran struck a preliminary deal to end the war in June, but President Donald Trump declared the agreement "over" on 8 July.

Why it matters: The exchange of strikes between the US and Iran has drawn in neighbouring Gulf states, with US allies in the region now being targeted by Iranian retaliatory attacks.

Source: BBC

3. Tate brothers arrested in US on new charges

Andrew Tate and his brother Tristan have been arrested in the US after British authorities announced further charges against them. The UK's Crown Prosecution Service said Andrew Tate was being charged with seven further counts of rape, as well as charges relating to alleged crimes including sex trafficking and child pornography, while Tristan Tate faces one count of sexual assault, two counts of rape and three counts of arranging or facilitating trafficking for sexual exploitation. The alleged offending is said to have taken place between July 2010 and August 2017, bringing the total number of alleged victims in the case to seven. The CPS said it has requested the extradition of the brothers, who are dual British-US citizens.

Why it matters: The US Marshals confirmed the Tates had been taken into custody pursuant to extradition proceedings, while their lawyer described the new charges as a "political hit" designed to counter a defamation action filed by the brothers in the US.

Source: BBC

4. Russia turns to cash amid wartime strain

Russians are returning to cash as mobile internet shutdowns disrupt card payments and businesses seek to dodge tax under mounting financial pressure. Russia has added 1.56 trillion roubles (£14.8bn; $20bn) in cash into circulation since the start of the year - the biggest rise for the equivalent period in any year outside the Covid-19 pandemic, according to Central Bank figures. In May, the Russian economy ministry cut its GDP growth forecast to 0.4% for 2026, putting the country on course for its weakest economic growth since 2022. About 6% of entrepreneurs said they had turned to "grey schemes" to cope with the new tax burden, according to a May survey by Russia's largest SME association, Opora Russia.

Why it matters: The shift to cash is making it harder for the state to collect tax, just as it faces a widening budget deficit and needs every rouble it can raise to fund the war in Ukraine.

Source: BBC

5. Burnham to scrap Starmer's digital ID plan

Incoming Prime Minister Andy Burnham is expected to scrap Keir Starmer's plans for digital ID cards in a "reset of priorities" when he enters Downing Street on Monday. Burnham's team said resources earmarked for the scheme would instead be redirected towards tackling the cost of living. A petition opposing the introduction of digital ID cards attracted about 3 million signatures when it was announced last year. The Office for Budget Responsibility had suggested the scheme could cost about £1.8bn, though its estimate was rejected by officials.

Why it matters: Sources close to Burnham said that, despite the decision, he remained committed to the previous government's crackdown on illegal working, which led to almost 9,000 arrests last year, with right-to-work checks expected to remain mandatory for all employers.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

Cairngorms wildfire crews make 'significant progress'

Fire crews have made "really significant progress" tackling the large-scale wildfire burning in the Cairngorms for four days, with more than 100 firefighters involved in the operation since the blaze started near Ryvoan Bothy, south of Nethy Bridge, on Wednesday. The RSPB said about 300 hectares of its Loch Garten reserve at Abernethy had been burned, with concerns raised over the wellbeing of capercaillie, a critically endangered woodland grouse thought to have been nesting nearby.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Companies House accounts filing reforms delayed to April 2028

From April 2028, all UK registered companies must file annual accounts in iXBRL format via commercial software, with web and paper-based filing systems closing for accounts filings. Small companies and micro-entities will be required to file profit and loss accounts but may opt out of publication on the public register; abridged accounts will no longer be permitted. Companies House states this revised date gives all companies one full accounting year plus 9 months (21 months) to prepare.

Source: Companies House
