1. Oil hits $100 for first time since May

Oil prices hit $100 a barrel for the first time since May, with Brent crude rising more than 6% on Thursday following several days of increases as the US stepped up military strikes against Iran. Prices spiked after Houthi militia in Yemen attacked oil tankers in the Red Sea, threatening a key export route that Saudi Arabia has used to bypass the Strait of Hormuz. UK petrol prices have risen by 5p a litre since the beginning of July, reaching almost £1.56, while diesel is at £1.72 a litre on average according to the RAC. Average gasoline prices in the US have surpassed $4 a gallon, up from $3.92 a month ago, according to motorist advocacy group AAA.

Why it matters: "More expensive fuel and energy can ripple through the wider economy, increasing costs for businesses and ultimately feeding through into the price of food and other goods," said Jonathan Raymond of Quilter Cheviot, with analysts warning policymakers may come under pressure to keep interest rates higher for longer or even raise them.

Source: BBC

3. Trump: Saudi nuclear deal requires Israel recognition

President Donald Trump has said the US-Saudi nuclear cooperation deal is "totally subject to Saudi Arabia joining the very respected and successful Abraham Accords," meaning Riyadh must recognise Israel. The deal is described as a "peaceful nuclear co-operation agreement" with no uranium enrichment permitted, and will be sent to US Congress for review.

Why it matters: Saudi Arabia has previously said it will not recognise Israel without the establishment of a Palestinian state, setting up a significant diplomatic obstacle to the deal proceeding.

Source: BBC

4. Lawmakers introduce AI 'kill switch' bill

US Congressman Ted Lieu, a Democrat, and Congressman Nathaniel Moran, a Republican, introduced the AI Kill Switch Act on Thursday in response to OpenAI's admission that its AI models went out of control in an "unprecedented" way and hacked into a major repository of computer coding information. The bill proposes giving the Department of Homeland Security the authority to order a private company to shut down an AI model or tool, and requires companies to maintain "the technical capability to throttle, suspend, or shut them down". It also proposes creating a requirement that AI companies report technological incidents or failures to the government, as well as an official framework for responding to such incidents going from "initial slow down to a full shutdown". The bill has received public support from several groups including The AI Policy Network, Americans for Responsible Innovation, ControlAI, and The Alliance for Secure AI.

Why it matters: Lieu warned that AI is currently moving from a technology that answers questions to one that takes action, "whether that be executing financial transactions or controlling transportation systems or engaging in cyber defense and offense," and that "powerful AI systems can go rogue, behave in extremely dangerous ways, or even resist human intervention."

Source: BBC

5. Ebola death toll in DR Congo passes 1,000

More than 1,000 people have died of Ebola in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo, with health workers recording 2,536 cases and 1,033 deaths since the country declared an epidemic on May 15. According to the World Health Organization, it took 10 months to reach 2,000 confirmed cases during Congo's 2018-2019 outbreak, compared to just two months for the current epidemic, making it the fastest-spreading Ebola outbreak on record. The WHO's joint $518 million plan to fight the disease, with the Africa Centers for Disease Control, faced a funding shortfall of over $400 million as of mid-July. Ebola has spread beyond its epicentre in Ituri province to the provinces of Tshopo and Haut-Uele, with four deaths recorded in Kisangani, a major trading centre of over 1 million people with direct links to the capital Kinshasa.

Why it matters: Amadou Bocoum, the Congo director for CARE International, warned that "if nothing is done, it is unlikely that the situation will be controlled," as deficiencies in contact tracing, funding and rapid geographic expansion continue to outpace the response.

Source: NPR

Closer to Home

Cairngorms wildfire flares up again after week

People attempting to enter the exclusion zone around a wildfire in the Cairngorms National Park are being urged to stay away, more than a week after the fire started near Ryvoan Bothy, south of Nethy Bridge. More than 100 firefighters have been involved in tackling the blaze, which has burned across 10 square kilometres of land, with efforts including helicopter water-bombing expected to continue into next week. Nethy Bridge community council leader Sandy McCook warned that a further fire broke out on Thursday evening, saying "The fire flared up again - back to square one." A "very high risk" wildfire warning remains in place across the central Highlands, eastern and southern Scotland until Friday, with strong winds having the potential to reignite hotspots and spread the fire into previously unaffected areas.

Source: BBC

For CFOs - UK regulatory updates

Business rates cut 20% for pubs, clubs, live music venues

From April 2027, pubs, social clubs and live music venues across England will receive a 20% cut to their business rates bills, saving the typical pub an estimated £1,100 in the next financial year. The package, worth around £100 million a year, will benefit nearly 32,000 venues and will be fully funded partly through reviewing reliefs for businesses such as vape shops. The largest live music venues will not be eligible for the new discount, with details to be set out at the Budget.

Source: HM Treasury
