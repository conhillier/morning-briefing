1. Twin quakes kill 32, devastate Caracas

Two back-to-back earthquakes struck northern Venezuela on Wednesday, with the first registering a magnitude of 7.2 and the second 7.5, reducing buildings to rubble in the capital Caracas. The epicentres, estimated to be about 3 miles apart, were near the town of Morón on Venezuela's Caribbean coast, some 100 miles west of Caracas.

Why it matters: The 7.5-magnitude quake is the largest earthquake to strike Venezuela since 1900, and USGS forecasting puts a 40% chance of a magnitude-6 or larger aftershock in the next week, with an almost certainty of a magnitude-5 or larger.

Source: NPR

2. Trump seeks $87bn for Iran war costs

The White House has asked lawmakers to approve $87.6bn (£66.5bn), mostly for "urgent needs" connected with the US war on Iran, a day after Congress passed a resolution rebuking the military action. The bulk of the funding - $67bn - is for the Defence Department, including $21bn for munitions, $17.3bn for operational costs and $12.1bn for classified programmes. The other money covers unrelated measures including $11bn for US farmers and $1.4bn to tackle the Ebola outbreak in Central Africa.

Why it matters: The proposal faces an uphill battle in Congress as the Iran conflict is unpopular with voters and midterm elections loom this November, while defence analysts and lawmakers say the Pentagon's existing $29bn cost estimate does not reflect the full scale of the conflict's financial damage.

Source: BBC

3. IAEA chief confirms Iran inspections will happen

The head of the International Atomic Energy Agency, Rafael Grossi, has confirmed that nuclear inspections in Iran will take place under the preliminary peace agreement with the US, saying "The inspections will indeed take place" and that the IAEA would work on "dates, procedures, places" very soon. Grossi said the memorandum of understanding signed by both presidents says "explicitly" that the dilution of Iran's highly enriched uranium would be carried out under IAEA supervision. However, Iranian Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem Gharibabadi pushed back, writing on X that access to Iran's damaged nuclear facilities and nuclear materials would only be addressed within the framework of a final agreement with the US and after practical steps had been taken to lift all sanctions. Under the 14-point memorandum of understanding, the US and Iran have committed to negotiating a final deal within 60 days.

Why it matters: The IAEA had previously been unable to provide any information on the current size, composition or whereabouts of Iran's stockpile of enriched uranium, and before the US-Israeli war Iran held 440kg of uranium enriched up to 60% purity - theoretically enough, if enriched to 90%, for as many as 10 bombs.

Source: BBC

4. Micron becomes tech's new margin king

Micron disclosed a gross margin of 84.9% in its fiscal third quarter, up from 74.9% in the prior period and 39% a year earlier, topping all major US tech companies including Meta at 81.9% and Nvidia at 75%. Revenue of $41.46 billion in the quarter was up more than $20 billion from the prior period, while net income of $28.24 billion is up over 100% from the previous high.

Why it matters: Micron projected a gross margin for the fiscal fourth quarter of roughly 86%, and CFO Murphy said the company expects "the market to remain tight beyond 2027", suggesting the pricing power for memory chips will persist.

Source: CNBC

5. France confirms first domestic Ebola case

France has confirmed its first case of Ebola in a doctor who returned from a humanitarian mission to an area affected by the outbreak in the Democratic Republic of the Congo. The patient was transferred to a specialist facility and was in a stable condition, while authorities are tracing contacts who will have to isolate at home for 21 days. The French health ministry said the risk to the general European public was very low. The current outbreak, centred on Ituri province in north-eastern DRC, had recorded 1,048 confirmed cases and 267 deaths as of 21 June, while neighbouring Uganda has recorded 20 cases and two deaths.

Why it matters: The current strain is the rare Bundibugyo virus, which has no vaccine or approved treatment, and modelling by the US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention suggested the outbreak could be the biggest on record.

Source: The Guardian

Closer to Home

UK's £750m supercomputer build begins in Midlothian

Construction has started on the UK's new £750m national supercomputer, to be hosted in University of Edinburgh buildings on the outskirts of Penicuik and Roslin in Midlothian, near the institute where Dolly the sheep was cloned. The project was originally shelved by the UK government when Labour came into power, then reinstated a year later after initially being deemed an "unfunded commitment". When finished at the end of next year, Prof Mark Parsons says it will be roughly the size of a medium-sized supermarket, capable of a billion-billion calculations per second, and will be 50 times more powerful than the existing ARCHER2 system on the same site. Government minister Kanishka Narayan told the BBC: "It is a huge moment for Scotland, and a huge moment for the UK."

Source: BBC
